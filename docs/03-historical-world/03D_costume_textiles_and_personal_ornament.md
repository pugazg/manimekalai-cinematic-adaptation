# 03D — Costume, Textiles and Personal Ornament

## Production Research Dossier 0.1

**Project:** *Manimekalai* cinematic adaptation  
**Scope:** Clothing, textile production, drape, ornament, hair, cosmetics, footwear, performance costume, ascetic appearance and character continuity  
**Status:** Working research edition. No final costume design is approved for fabrication.

---

## 1. Purpose

This dossier establishes a disciplined costume language for the film without turning later stage traditions, medieval sculpture, modern cinema or generic “ancient India” imagery into false evidence for the world of *Manimekalai*.

The surviving evidence is uneven. The literary text repeatedly describes flowers, bangles, ornaments, hair, eyes, garlands, bodily beauty, mendicant appearance and changes of form. Archaeology preserves beads, bangles, ear ornaments, gold fragments, metal objects and spindle whorls more readily than cloth itself. It does not preserve a complete Puhar wardrobe.

The costume department must therefore distinguish between:

- what the epic says about appearance;
- what early historic Tamil archaeology demonstrates about production and ornament materials;
- what comparative evidence makes plausible;
- what the film must reconstruct for dramatic clarity;
- and what cannot be claimed as historically exact.

### Binding methodological rule

> An ornament excavated in early historic Tamilakam may support the use of its material or broad object category, but it does not automatically establish the exact costume, gender, rank, body position or ritual meaning assigned to it in the film.

---

## 2. Evidence categories

- **[TEXT]** Directly stated or strongly implied in *Manimekalai*.
- **[CROSS]** Supported by another early Tamil literary text after edition-level verification.
- **[HISTORY]** Supported by archaeology, inscriptions, numismatics or specialist material-culture research.
- **[INTERPRETATION]** A controlled production reconstruction required because evidence is incomplete.
- **[CAUTION]** A visual convention that must not be presented as established fact.

### Evidence limitation

Organic textiles rarely survive in the humid archaeological environments relevant to Tamilakam. Spindle whorls demonstrate thread production, but by themselves they do not identify the fibre, colour, weave, loom type, garment cut or social identity of the wearer.

Accordingly, the dossier may authorize a **range** of plausible costume systems while withholding approval from any design marketed as “the exact dress of Sangam-age Puhar.”

# Part I — Appearance inside *Manimekalai*

## 3. Manimekalai as an ornamented young woman

**[TEXT]** The epic repeatedly describes Manimekalai through ornamented hair, flowers, bangles, luminous skin and facial features. In Chapter 12 she approaches Aravana Adigal with dark hair adorned with flowers and is addressed as a woman wearing golden bangles. Elsewhere her wrists are described as decorated with bangles, and she is repeatedly called ornamented or adorned.

These descriptions do not mean that she must wear every available ornament in every scene. Classical poetic description can express beauty, youth, rank, erotic attention and auspiciousness rather than provide a literal inventory.

### Production implication

Manimekalai’s early costume should communicate that she comes from a prosperous artistic household and has been trained to be socially visible. Her ornament should be refined rather than royal and should never make her look like a modern bridal model.

Approved preliminary elements may include:

- a draped lower garment suitable for movement;
- a light upper wrap or breast covering only where the selected historical interpretation supports it;
- flower dressing integrated into the hair;
- a limited wrist-bangle set;
- ear ornament;
- one or two neck or waist elements rather than a full jewellery display;
- cosmetic definition around the eyes;
- practical barefoot movement or simple footwear depending on location.

**[CAUTION]** The exact number, order, attachment method and design of garments remain unapproved until the original Tamil terminology and cross-textual evidence are reviewed.

## 4. Flowers are costume, fragrance and social signal

**[TEXT]** Flowers are worn in Manimekalai’s hair, used in garlands, associated with goddesses and princes, and woven into the emotional world of the flower garden. In Chapter 21 Manimekalai’s hair is described as adorned with flowers dripping with honey. Udayakumaran and sacred images are also associated with flower garlands.

Flowers should not be treated as a decorative afterthought. They affect:

- colour;
- fragrance;
- insects;
- freshness and wilting;
- time of day;
- social preparation;
- sensuality;
- ritual status;
- emotional continuity.

### Production rule

Every major flower arrangement must have:

1. a named or researchable botanical source;
2. a seasonal justification;
3. a method of attachment;
4. a continuity lifespan;
5. a social or ritual purpose.

Random modern jasmine strings may not be used as a universal shorthand for every woman, every city and every occasion.

## 5. Bangles and the wrist

**[TEXT]** Bangles are a recurring part of Manimekalai’s physical description. In Chapter 18 she reflects on the prince holding her wrist decorated with bangles.

The wrist has dramatic value because it is where desire, restraint, identity and renunciation meet. A bangle can sound before a face is seen; it can mark wealth, movement and gendered attention; its removal or reduction can show a change in life without dialogue.

### Production rule

Bangle materials must vary by character and status. Archaeology supports shell, glass, copper and other materials in early historic Tamil contexts. Gold is possible for elite or specially significant pieces but must not become the default material.

The soundtrack should record distinct bangle sounds for:

- shell;
- glass;
- thin metal;
- heavier metal;
- mixed sets.

## 6. Eyes, hair and cosmetics

**[TEXT]** Chapter 18 describes Manimekalai through long fish-like eyes marked with kohl, curved eyebrows, pearl-like teeth, coral-red lips and a moon-like face. These are poetic conventions as well as appearance cues.

### Approved interpretation

- Eye definition may be used for elite, artistic and ceremonial characters.
- Cosmetic application should vary in density and finish.
- Hair should be dressed with oils, braids, knots, loose sections, flowers or ornaments according to occupation and scene.
- Weather, travel, grief, imprisonment and renunciation must visibly alter grooming.

**[CAUTION]** Modern salon symmetry, false eyelashes, high-definition bridal makeup, contouring and uniformly glossy skin are prohibited.

## 7. The “thin bhikshuni” description

**[TEXT]** In the supplied English translation of Chapter 18, Udayakumaran sees Manimekalai “dressed like a thin bhikshuni doing tapas.” The wording provides a strong dramatic contrast between her earlier ornamented visibility and her mendicant path.

**[CAUTION]** The exact underlying Tamil word, garment terminology and institutional meaning must be verified before the costume department creates a standardized Buddhist nun’s uniform.

The line may indicate one or more of the following:

- reduced ornament;
- a lean body shaped by discipline;
- plain or restrained clothing;
- a social appearance recognizable as mendicant;
- the prince’s perception of her transformed status.

It does **not** by itself prove a particular robe colour, robe construction, shaved head, stitched garment or pan-Buddhist uniform.

## 8. Disguise as Kāyasandihai

**[TEXT]** Manimekalai takes the form of Kāyasandihai and becomes unrecognizable to Udayakumaran and Kanchanan. She later announces the removal of that form and returns to her own appearance.

The disguise is not a mere change of dress; it is a transformed embodied identity. Costume, hair, body movement, voice, ornament and silhouette should all contribute.

### Screen treatment

The transformation should avoid a modern superhero costume-change effect. A credible approach is to establish Kāyasandihai’s visual language in advance and then allow Manimekalai’s body, costume and movement to resolve into that known identity.

The production must determine whether the transformation is shown:

- fully and literally;
- through subjective perception;
- through ritual action and match cuts;
- or through partial visual ambiguity.

No final decision is approved in this dossier.

# Part II — Archaeological foundations

## 9. Evidence for textile production

**[HISTORY]** The Tamil Nadu Department of Archaeology reports spindle whorls at Keeladi and identifies one explicitly as a weaver’s tool. Spindle whorls also occur in wider early historic Tamil archaeological contexts.

This supports thread production and the presence of textile work. It does not establish:

- a single loom type;
- a single fibre;
- centralised state workshops;
- the gender of every spinner or weaver;
- the exact fineness of elite cloth;
- a specific garment pattern.

### Production reconstruction

The film may show:

- fibre preparation;
- hand spinning;
- winding thread;
- weaving in domestic or workshop settings;
- cloth measuring;
- dye or washing processes only where researched;
- repair and reuse;
- merchants handling folded cloth.

A loom design must not be finalized until an archaeological textile specialist reviews available South Indian evidence.

## 10. Ornament materials at Keeladi

**[HISTORY]** Official Keeladi excavation summaries report:

- terracotta beads;
- glass beads;
- micro-glass beads;
- semi-precious stone beads including agate, carnelian and crystal;
- ear ornaments;
- shell and glass bangles;
- gold ornaments or fragments;
- golden wire;
- copper objects.

These finds support material diversity and differences between simple and lavish ornamentation.

### Production implication

The costume world should use a hierarchy of material, workmanship and access rather than a simple division between “poor people with no jewellery” and “rich people covered in gold.”

A wealthy merchant may display rare stones. A working woman may wear shell, terracotta, glass or inherited metal. An ascetic may retain one practical or identity-bearing object. A royal household may control larger, finer or more technically complex pieces.

## 11. Pattaraiperumbudur evidence

**[HISTORY]** Excavations at Pattaraiperumbudur produced carnelian beads, shell bangles, glass beads, copper objects, an ivory ornament and other household objects in deposits that include early historic material.

This supports broad access to multiple ornament materials across Tamil regions, but the site is not Puhar and its assemblage cannot be copied as a complete Puhar costume kit.

## 12. Kodumanal and bead production

**[HISTORY]** Kodumanal yielded beads of quartz and clay, and a burial assemblage containing hundreds of carnelian beads. The site is important for craft and exchange.

### Production implication

Beads should vary in:

- size;
- drilling precision;
- polish;
- translucence;
- colour variation;
- stringing pattern;
- wear;
- repair;
- ownership history.

Perfectly uniform modern beads are prohibited unless the prop is specifically meant to demonstrate elite specialist manufacture.

## 13. Teriruveli and conch working

**[HISTORY]** Teriruveli yielded shell bangles, sawn conches and carnelian beads associated with Sangam-period material.

This supports the possibility of visible stages of shell ornament manufacture:

- raw conch;
- cut sections;
- rough rings;
- polished bangles;
- breakage and reuse.

Shell ornaments should not appear as plastic-white uniform props. They should show natural colour, growth lines, cutting marks, polishing differences and wear.

## 14. What archaeology does not yet provide

The present working evidence does not authorize certainty about:

- the exact form of Manimekalai’s lower garment;
- whether every elite woman wore a stitched upper garment;
- the dominant cloth fibre in each scene;
- the use of silk in a specific costume;
- standardized colour symbolism;
- a single Chola court uniform;
- a single Buddhist robe colour;
- gender-exclusive ornaments in every context;
- footwear for each social group;
- body tattooing or painted marks;
- exact royal crowns;
- the later *devadasi* costume system;
- the later Bharatanatyam fan costume;
- medieval Chola bronze jewellery as direct evidence for the film’s period.

# Part III — Production costume system

## 15. General silhouette principles

**[INTERPRETATION]** The film will use drape, wrap, tie, fold, pin, belt, cord and ornament rather than defaulting to highly tailored modern garments.

This is not a prohibition on all stitching. It is a rule against unexamined tailoring conventions.

Costume must respond to:

- heat and humidity;
- monsoon and sea wind;
- physical labour;
- travel;
- dance;
- ritual;
- rank;
- privacy;
- age;
- grief;
- bodily discipline.

### Silhouette families

1. **Harbour labour:** short, secure, washable drapes; reduced loose ornament; head or shoulder cloth where functional.
2. **Merchant household:** finer cloth, layered drape, controlled ornament, practical display of wealth.
3. **Performance household:** movement-responsive textiles, trained grooming, flowers, sound-producing ornaments, but not later Bharatanatyam costume.
4. **Royal court:** greater volume, higher-quality materials, guarded ornament systems, insignia and controlled repetition.
5. **Religious specialist:** institution-specific restraint or markers, pending sectarian research.
6. **Mendicant and ascetic:** practical, repaired, reduced and weathered clothing, without making every tradition visually identical.
7. **Foreign visitor:** subtle differences in drape, cloth finish, accessories and grooming; no caricature.
8. **Marginal or displaced person:** repaired, reused, mismatched and climate-worn clothing; poverty must not become decorative distress styling.

## 16. Colour policy

No universal “ancient earth-tone palette” is approved.

The film may use:

- undyed and lightly coloured everyday cloth;
- stronger colour for elite, performance or ritual use;
- faded and repeatedly washed versions of the same hues;
- trade-linked colour variation;
- flower and ornament colour as part of the costume composition.

**[CAUTION]** Indigo, madder, turmeric, saffron, lac and other dye associations require material or textual verification before they are assigned as historically specific costume dyes.

Colour must be controlled by character, availability, status and continuity—not by modern assumptions that all ancient people wore beige or that every religious identity had a fixed colour code.

## 17. Cloth quality and wear

Costume distinction should depend as much on quality as on colour:

- yarn fineness;
- density;
- transparency;
- softness;
- drape;
- border treatment;
- repair;
- fading;
- sweat absorption;
- salt exposure;
- storage folds;
- ceremonial freshness.

Puhar’s wealthy streets may contain fine cloth, but labourers, travellers and mendicants must show repeated use. Cloth should behave physically: cling in rain, lift in sea wind, darken with sweat, crease under belts, and fray at working edges.

## 18. Ornament hierarchy

The costume department will classify every ornament by:

- material;
- manufacturing difficulty;
- source or trade route;
- owner’s status;
- body location;
- sound;
- weight;
- risk during movement;
- emotional meaning;
- inheritance or gift history.

### Preliminary material families

- terracotta;
- shell and conch;
- glass;
- copper or copper alloy;
- iron where appropriate;
- silver where evidenced;
- gold and gold wire;
- ivory;
- quartz;
- agate;
- carnelian;
- crystal;
- pearls, pending source-specific verification;
- flowers, fibre cords and organic materials.

**[CAUTION]** A poetic comparison to gold, pearl or coral is not automatically a literal material statement.

## 19. Hair systems

Hair design must be character-specific.

Possible variables include:

- centre or side division;
- oiling;
- braid number;
- knot height;
- loose hair;
- flower placement;
- pins, cords or bead elements;
- age-related greying;
- ritual restraint;
- travel disorder;
- grief-related neglect.

### Manimekalai

Her hair should move through a visual arc:

1. controlled artistic-household grooming;
2. flower-garden beauty under public gaze;
3. wind-disordered Manipallavam isolation;
4. restrained service and begging;
5. transformed Kāyasandihai identity;
6. grief and exhaustion after Udayakumaran’s death;
7. increasing simplicity during philosophical and ascetic stages.

This arc must not be reduced to “ornamented hair, then shaved head” without textual and institutional evidence.

## 20. Footwear

No single footwear rule is approved.

Production research must determine plausible use of:

- bare feet;
- simple sandals;
- protective footwear for travel, military or labour contexts;
- ceremonial removal of footwear;
- indoor and sacred-space practice.

Modern leather sandals, decorative Kolhapuri-style footwear and later royal shoes must not be used without review.

# Part IV — Character costume continuity

## 21. Manimekalai

### Phase A — Daughter of Madhavi

- refined household cloth;
- modest but visible ornament;
- flowers and groomed hair;
- wrist bangles as an important motif;
- colours connected to youth and the performance world without dressing her as a stage dancer.

### Phase B — Flower garden and pursuit

- costume designed for walking and flower gathering;
- enough social visibility for Udayakumaran’s recognition;
- ornament sound and hair flowers used in suspense;
- no impractical ceremonial excess.

### Phase C — Manipallavam

- same garment system affected by wind, salt, sleep and travel;
- loosened flowers;
- sand on cloth and skin;
- visual isolation;
- no instant spotless mystical costume.

### Phase D — Amudhasurabhi service

- reduced ornament;
- practical drape;
- secure way to carry and serve from the vessel;
- cloth that permits kneeling, walking and feeding;
- recognizably transformed social role.

### Phase E — Kāyasandihai form

- new silhouette, movement, grooming and ornament logic;
- exact relation to the known Kāyasandihai design;
- continuity that permits the audience to understand Kanchanan’s error.

### Phase F — imprisonment and court

- costume affected by confinement;
- no glamorized prison styling;
- reduced access to grooming;
- royal control over her physical presentation may become part of the conflict.

### Phase G — Vanji and Kanchi

- increasingly restrained wardrobe;
- travel wear;
- visual attention transferred from beauty to presence, speech and action;
- simplicity without erasing dignity or colour.

## 22. Madhavi

Madhavi must never be dressed as a later Bharatanatyam dancer.

Her costume must communicate:

- elite artistic training;
- control of gesture and presentation;
- past public fame;
- wealth and patronage history;
- present withdrawal and grief;
- an embodied memory of performance even when she no longer performs.

Her renunciation should not look like modern widow styling unless evidence supports it. The contrast may be shown through reduced grooming, altered cloth quality, fewer flowers, quieter ornament and changed posture.

## 23. Sudhamathi

Sudhamathi should be visually distinct from Manimekalai without being reduced to a plain companion.

Her design may show:

- lower household status or different personal history;
- practical movement;
- fewer or different ornaments;
- strong continuity across travel and crisis;
- visual reliability.

Her costume should support her function as observer, protector and emotional bridge.

## 24. Chitrāpathi

Chitrāpathi represents the institution and economic logic of the performance household more aggressively than Madhavi.

Her costume may use:

- carefully maintained ornament;
- stronger public presentation;
- older but authoritative fashion;
- controlled sensuality;
- signs of wealth linked to patronage.

She must not become a caricatured brothel madam or a later court-dancer stereotype.

## 25. Udayakumaran

The prince’s clothing should establish rank through quality, fit of drape, guards, grooming, weapons, flower garlands and controlled ornament—not through a giant fantasy crown.

His visual arc may move from:

- festival splendour;
- hunting or chariot practicality;
- private obsession;
- increasingly disordered restraint;
- final vulnerability.

His costume must allow action and must not resemble later imperial Chola sculpture by default.

## 26. Queen Rājamādevi

The queen’s wardrobe should communicate institutional power, maternal grief and controlled access to luxury.

Design principles:

- repeated royal motifs rather than random jewellery overload;
- attendants participating in dressing;
- weight and formality in public scenes;
- reduced or altered presentation during grief;
- costume used as political armour.

## 27. Aravana Adigal

**[TEXT]** Aravana Adigal is old, with white hair and a trembling tongue.

His costume should show age, practice, repair and institutional identity without using generic saffron robes. Cloth colour, wrapping method, carried objects and footwear require Buddhist-historical review.

## 28. Aputhiran

Aputhiran’s appearance should support his ethical identity rather than turn him into a fantasy saint.

Potential visual characteristics:

- practical traveller or mendicant clothing;
- visible wear from feeding and travel;
- reduced ornament;
- bodily fatigue;
- a functional relation to the Amudhasurabhi.

## 29. Kāyasandihai and Kanchanan

Their costume system should suggest a different social and perhaps celestial-geographical world without defaulting to glittering fantasy armour.

Before design approval, the project must determine:

- whether their difference is primarily material, silhouette, ornament, movement or light;
- how much their costume relates to Puhar conventions;
- how Kanchanan recognizes the disguised Manimekalai;
- what visual evidence makes his mistaken conclusion emotionally credible.

## 30. Foreign merchants and visitors

Puhar is multilingual and cosmopolitan, but foreign costume must be used cautiously.

Rules:

- no costume may be based solely on modern nationality;
- no Roman toga parade;
- no uniform “Arab merchant” look;
- no comic accent costume;
- difference should often be subtle: cloth weave, fastening, head covering, belt, pouch, footwear, beard treatment or carried goods.

Foreign-looking props must have route and date notes.

# Part V — Performance costume

## 31. Madhavi’s inherited performance world

The film must reconstruct a sophisticated Tamil performance culture without using the codified visual package of modern Bharatanatyam as direct historical evidence.

Prohibited by default:

- stitched fan-front dance costume;
- modern temple-jewellery sets;
- modern stage makeup;
- standardized salangai presentation copied without research;
- modern recital hair and flower arrangement;
- later nationalist “classical dancer” iconography.

### What may be retained after research

Some underlying principles may be historically plausible even when modern form is not:

- costume designed to reveal rhythmic movement;
- sound-producing ankle or body ornaments;
- controlled hair and flower design;
- expressive eye treatment;
- performance-specific cloth management;
- distinction between rehearsal and public presentation.

Every performance element must be traced through early literature, archaeology and performance-history review.

## 32. Movement tests

Before approval, each performance costume must pass:

1. walking test;
2. seated posture test;
3. turn and spin test;
4. arm-extension test;
5. floor-level movement test;
6. sweat and heat test;
7. flower-retention test;
8. ornament-sound recording;
9. emergency-release and actor-safety test.

Historical appearance cannot be used to justify unsafe weight or restricted breathing.

# Part VI — Department workflow

Every hero costume requires a card with:

- scene range;
- garment components;
- textile hypothesis;
- dye hypothesis;
- ornament materials;
- hair design;
- cosmetics;
- footwear;
- source evidence IDs;
- adaptation decision IDs;
- uncertainty statement;
- specialist reviewer;
- movement test status;
- continuity photographs.

## 34. Prop and jewellery provenance

Every prominent ornament must have:

- material specification;
- manufacturing technique;
- evidence source;
- body attachment method;
- weight;
- sound;
- duplicate and stunt versions;
- ageing plan;
- repair history;
- screen ownership history.

No mass-produced jewellery set should be accepted merely because it looks “traditional.”

## 35. Costume ageing levels

Approved continuity levels:

- newly prepared ceremonial;
- normal household use;
- work-worn;
- sea-wind and salt affected;
- rain-soaked;
- travel-worn;
- fasting or ascetic use;
- prison-worn;
- famine-world repair;
- grief-neglected.

Distressing must arise from story action and material behaviour, not generic brown dye and torn hems.

## 36. Crowd costume design

Crowd design must be statistical rather than individually spectacular.

For each district, define:

- dominant garment families;
- material range;
- ornament frequency;
- colour distribution;
- age variation;
- occupation markers;
- foreign-visitor percentage;
- festival enhancement;
- poverty and repair levels.

Repeated rental costumes, identical turbans and matching jewellery sets are prohibited.

# Part VII — Binding visual prohibitions

The following rules are binding until revised by evidence:

1. No modern Bharatanatyam fan costume for Madhavi or her artistic community.
2. No medieval imperial Chola crown or bronze-jewellery package used as automatic early historic evidence.
3. No universal sari-blouse costume imposed on all women.
4. No universal dhoti-and-angavastram uniform imposed on all men.
5. No standardized saffron robe for all ascetics.
6. No standardized Buddhist nun costume without philological and historical review.
7. No all-gold elite population.
8. No plastic-looking shell, glass or stone ornaments.
9. No modern bridal makeup or salon hair.
10. No exact dye claim without evidence.
11. No foreign visitor caricature.
12. No poverty represented only through dirty brown cloth.
13. No random body marks, caste marks or religious symbols without source review.
14. No footwear copied from modern ethnic-fashion catalogues.
15. No publicity claim that a reconstructed costume is “exact Sangam dress.”

# Part IX — Preliminary scene applications

## 40. Festival dawn

The opening should establish costume diversity through function:

- harbour workers securing short drapes;
- merchants in finer but travel-practical cloth;
- officials preparing ceremonial layers;
- performers dressing and arranging flowers;
- religious teachers with distinct but not exaggerated identities;
- visitors whose differences are noticed through detail rather than stereotype.

## 41. Madhavi’s silent house

The city is dressing for spectacle while Madhavi’s household withdraws.

The visual contrast may include:

- unused performance ornaments;
- flowers not worn;
- folded textiles from an earlier life;
- Manimekalai’s partially completed garland;
- Madhavi’s reduced presentation;
- Chitrāpathi’s insistence on public appearance.

## 42. Flower garden

Manimekalai’s flowers, bangles, cloth movement and hair become part of the prince’s perception. The scene must remain emotionally complex: the same visual beauty that marks her social world also makes her vulnerable to pursuit.

## 43. Feeding the hungry

Costume must show transformation through function:

- reduced ornament;
- practical garment management;
- repeated kneeling and serving;
- sweat and food contact;
- crowd clothing showing multiple forms of hardship;
- the prince’s memory of her earlier appearance colliding with her present role.

## 44. Kāyasandihai misrecognition

The costume design must make three things simultaneously credible:

1. the audience understands Manimekalai has changed form;
2. Udayakumaran still seeks Manimekalai beneath the disguise;
3. Kanchanan believes he sees his wife.

This requires pre-established Kāyasandihai costume language, not a last-minute mask or colour change.

## 45. Kanchi famine

Costume should reveal systems of scarcity:

- cloth repeatedly patched;
- ornaments sold or absent;
- children wearing altered adult garments;
- elite garments preserved behind guarded spaces;
- mendicants and displaced people with different histories rather than one distressed uniform.

# 47. Working conclusion

The film’s costume world should be materially rich, socially differentiated and visually specific, but it must remain honest about uncertainty.

*Manimekalai* gives us flowers, bangles, hair, kohl, garlands, beauty, restraint, mendicancy and transformation. Archaeology gives us spindle whorls, shell and glass bangles, ear ornaments, gold wire, terracotta and semi-precious beads, carnelian, quartz, ivory and copper. The production task is to connect those bodies of evidence through controlled design—not to fill the gaps with later dance costume, medieval Chola imagery or generic cinematic fantasy.

Manimekalai’s visual arc will not be a simplistic movement from “jewelled woman” to “colourless nun.” It will be the gradual transfer of meaning from ornament and public gaze to ethical action, presence and self-command.
