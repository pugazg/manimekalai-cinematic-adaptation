# 03I — Economy, Labour, Trade Guilds and Material Culture

## Production Research Dossier 0.1

**Project:** *Manimekalai* cinematic adaptation  
**Scope:** Work, markets, merchants, artisans, performers, transport, valuation, exchange, production chains, material culture, economic vulnerability and the limited evidence for early corporate merchant organisation  
**Status:** Working research edition. No exact wage, tax, price, coin-conversion, guild constitution or complete market plan is approved.

---

## 1. Purpose

This dossier establishes the economic world of *Manimekalai* as a system of labour and interdependence rather than a background display of luxury goods.

The epic’s cities contain merchants, grain sellers, weavers, valuers of gold, dealers in precious stones, makers working with shell and conch, pearl-garland makers, dancers, royal servants, mahouts, horse trainers, ministers, religious specialists, beggars and people who feed strangers. Maritime voyages require crews, pilots, provisioning and risk. Performance requires years of training. Public feeding depends on invisible service labour. Hunger reveals who has access to work, property and social protection.

The production must therefore ask of every visible object:

- who obtained the raw material;
- who transformed it;
- what tools were required;
- who transported it;
- who valued or authenticated it;
- who owned it;
- who sold or gifted it;
- who repaired, cleaned or discarded it;
- and who carried the bodily risk of the work.

### Binding methodological rule

> An imported object proves movement of material or technology, not the ethnicity of its user, the identity of the ship that carried it, or a complete foreign commercial colony.

---

## 2. Evidence categories

- **[TEXT]** Directly stated or strongly implied in *Manimekalai*.
- **[CROSS]** Supported by another early Tamil literary source after edition-level verification.
- **[HISTORY]** Supported by archaeology, inscriptions, numismatics or specialist research.
- **[INTERPRETATION]** A controlled production reconstruction required because evidence is incomplete.
- **[CAUTION]** A familiar economic image that must not be presented as established fact.

### Evidence limitation

The epic is neither an account book nor a legal code. Archaeology preserves pottery, beads, tools, slag, coins, inscriptions and structures more readily than wages, contracts, debt, household bargaining or the status of workers. The film may create a coherent economy, but it must not advertise invented details as documented Sangam-period institutions.

# Part I — The economic world inside *Manimekalai*

## 3. The author’s merchant identity

**[TEXT]** The colophon identifies Chathanar as a *kūlavāṇikan*, rendered in the supplied translation as a grain merchant.

This does not make every economic description documentary reportage, but it is significant that the epic’s authorial identity is associated with commerce rather than solely with court or monastery.

### Production consequence

Economic activity should be observed with specificity. Grain is not a generic sack. It moves through cultivation, drying, storage, measuring, transport, retail, cooking and relief. Merchant characters should understand loss, spoilage, creditworthiness, travel risk and reputation even when the screenplay does not explain these concepts aloud.

## 4. Occupational streets and specialised work

**[TEXT]** Chapter 28 describes urban streets associated with:

- shell and conch working;
- pearl-garland making;
- dancers serving royal and public audiences;
- grain selling;
- royalty and officials;
- time announcers using bells;
- women providing sexual or courtly entertainment;
- weavers producing colourful cloth;
- specialists assessing the value of gold;
- merchants dealing in precious diamonds;
- ritual specialists;
- ministers and royal personnel;
- mahouts training elephants;
- trainers of horses.

The passage presents a city organised partly through occupational concentration and specialised knowledge.

**[CAUTION]** It does not prove that every occupation occupied a permanently segregated street, that all practitioners belonged to hereditary corporations, or that the listed arrangement applies identically to Puhar, Vanji and Kanchi.

### Screen principle

Occupational identity should be visible through repeated activity rather than signs explaining a trade. A viewer should recognise a shell-working lane through dust, sawn fragments, soaking, cutting and sound; a textile lane through fibre, winding, tension, dye, drying and folded stock; and a valuation space through controlled light, touchstones, balances, witnesses and guarded handling.

## 5. Performance is skilled labour

**[TEXT]** Chapter 2 lists a demanding curriculum associated with trained performing women, including multiple forms of dance and song, rhythm, instruments, languages, ball play, culinary knowledge, cosmetics, bathing, seasonal conduct, bodily technique, interpretation, concealed calculation, painting, flower selection and arrangement, design, jewellery organisation and knowledge of time.

The list presents performance as learned, disciplined and multidisciplinary work.

### Binding production principle

Madhavi, Chitrapathi, Vasanthamalai and the women around them must not be treated as decorative courtesans who simply possess natural beauty. Their world requires:

- teachers and rehearsal;
- musicians and instrument maintenance;
- costume and flower workers;
- physical conditioning;
- memory and improvisation;
- patron negotiation;
- public reputation;
- income and household management;
- vulnerability to royal and elite desire.

The film must distinguish artistic authority from the social constraints placed on performers.

## 6. Merchants, ships and distributed risk

**[TEXT]** The epic presents merchants travelling by ship, merchant groups reaching Manipallavam, valuable cargo associated with wrecks, and voyages that can end in separation, abandonment or death.

Merchant wealth is therefore inseparable from:

- sailors and pilots;
- loading and unloading labour;
- packers and warehouse workers;
- food and water suppliers;
- rope, sail and vessel repair;
- interpreters and brokers;
- guards or escorts where evidenced;
- families waiting for return;
- creditors, partners or patrons only where later research supports them.

### Screen rule

A merchant must not be shown as producing wealth alone. Every successful voyage rests on visible and invisible workers, and every wreck distributes loss unevenly among owners, crew, passengers, dependants and labourers.

## 7. Household exchange, alms and social protection

**[TEXT]** Manimekalai’s begging and Athirai’s gift show food moving from a household to a mendicant and then toward wider public service. Sudhamathi’s story shows how injury and loss of livelihood can push a household into begging. The Amudhasurabhi episodes show that public relief grows from repeated relationships among donors, servers and recipients.

### Economic meaning

The adaptation should distinguish:

- reciprocal hospitality;
- religious alms;
- emergency charity;
- organised public feeding;
- household dependence;
- royal provisioning;
- and access to livelihood.

These are not interchangeable forms of generosity.

# Part II — Archaeology of production and exchange

## 8. Kodumanal: inland craft and route node

**[HISTORY]** Kodumanal preserves early historic and megalithic material including inscribed and graffiti-marked pottery, quartz and clay beads, carnelian assemblages, iron weapons and other metal objects. Specialist research places the site on an important overland route and identifies substantial craft production.

### Production use

Kodumanal provides a comparative model for:

- raw stone selection;
- bead shaping, drilling and polishing;
- failed and unfinished products;
- ironworking and tool maintenance;
- storage of small high-value goods;
- movement between inland production and coastal exchange;
- workers sharing technical knowledge across sites.

**[CAUTION]** Kodumanal is not a direct plan for one Puhar workshop. Its evidence should inform technologies and labour sequences, not be transplanted room-for-room.

## 9. Karur: inland trade, political power and valuation

**[HISTORY]** Excavations at Karur have produced Tamil-Brahmi inscribed sherds, amphora fragments, rouletted ware, coins, precious stones, brick remains and iron objects. Finds from the Amaravati riverbed include inscribed rings and other valuable objects.

The assemblage supports Karur’s role as an inland political and commercial centre connected to regional and long-distance exchange.

### Production use

Vanji’s economy may include:

- guarded movement of high-value small goods;
- stone and metal specialists;
- court demand interacting with merchant supply;
- river crossings and caravan arrival;
- brokers connecting inland producers with coastal merchants;
- writing and personal marks on portable objects.

## 10. Alagankulam and coastal circulation

**[HISTORY]** Alagankulam has yielded Mediterranean-region pottery, amphora fragments, rouletted ware, Tamil-Brahmi sherds, beads, bricks and coins. Scientific work also shows varied local and circulating ceramic production in early historic Tamil Nadu.

### Binding caution

Imported pottery does not prove that every vessel, merchant, shopkeeper or customer was Roman. Goods may move through many intermediaries and may be reused long after their first journey.

The film must not turn the port into a Roman bazaar simply because amphorae and coins exist in the archaeological record.

## 11. Arikamedu and specialised bead production

**[HISTORY]** Research at Arikamedu identifies extensive glass and stone bead manufacture involving multiple specialised operations. Comparative research across Arikamedu, Kodumanal and other sites argues for connected communities of practice rather than simple ethnic ownership of technologies.

### Screen principle

Craft identity should arise through technique:

- sequence of drilling and polishing;
- heat management;
- material sorting;
- hand and eye training;
- tool wear;
- apprenticeship;
- cooperation between specialists.

A distinctive product does not automatically identify the worker’s ethnicity.

## 12. Writing, names and economic trust

**[HISTORY]** Tamil-Brahmi appears on pottery, rings, seals and other portable objects across early historic Tamilakam, including finds associated with trade centres. Personal names and marks show that writing could participate in ownership, identity, donation, movement or record practices.

### Caution

A name on an object does not by itself reveal whether it marked:

- the maker;
- owner;
- merchant;
- recipient;
- donor;
- workshop;
- contents;
- or a later user.

The prop department may use marks only through a documented system approved by epigraphists. Decorative pseudo-Tamil-Brahmi is prohibited.

# Part III — Guilds and corporate organisation

## 13. What can be said about early guilds

**[HISTORY]** The Tamil Nadu Department of Archaeology notes stray Tamil-Brahmi references such as the Vellarai *nigamaththor*, indicating some form of regional merchant or corporate identity in the early period.

This is important but limited evidence.

### Binding rule

The film may show:

- merchants travelling in groups;
- shared warehouses or meeting places;
- collective reputation;
- witnesses and guarantors;
- pooled information;
- coordinated transport;
- local associations where evidence permits.

It may not automatically show the fully developed institutions known from much later inscriptions.

## 14. Later guilds must remain later

**[CAUTION]** Ayyavole/Ainnurruvar, Nanadesi, Manigramam and other extensively documented corporate guild systems belong primarily to later centuries in their epigraphically visible forms.

The production must not import without evidence:

- medieval guild banners;
- military escorts presented as guild armies;
- formal charters copied from later inscriptions;
- later corporate titles;
- autonomous merchant quarters governed like city-states;
- medieval temple-guild relationships.

The existence of early trade does not prove that later institutional forms already existed unchanged.

# Part IV — Exchange, value and money

## 15. A mixed economy

**[HISTORY/INTERPRETATION]** Coins, imported ceramics, precious stones, inscribed objects and specialised production support complex exchange. They do not establish a fully monetised economy in which every transaction uses coins.

The screen economy should allow multiple forms of value transfer:

- weighed metal;
- coins;
- measured grain;
- finished goods;
- gifts;
- alms;
- service obligations;
- patronage;
- reciprocal hospitality;
- credit or deferred settlement only when supported by specialist reconstruction.

### Binding caution

No modern price labels, universal coin denominations or exact exchange-rate dialogue may be used before numismatic and metrological review.

## 16. Valuation is labour

**[TEXT]** Chapter 28 identifies specialists who assess gold and merchants dealing in precious stones.

The film should show valuation as skilled and contested work involving:

- weighing;
- testing surfaces;
- examining flaws;
- comparing colour and transparency;
- checking workmanship;
- observing marks and seals;
- establishing witnesses;
- negotiating trust.

The valuer is not merely a shopkeeper. Their judgement can redistribute wealth.

## 17. Measures and containers

Every market scene requires a controlled system for:

- dry measures;
- liquid measures;
- weights;
- balances;
- storage jars;
- baskets;
- cloth bundles;
- seals and closures;
- counting or marking.

**[CAUTION]** The exact names, capacities and conversion relationships remain unapproved until original Tamil terminology, inscriptions and archaeological finds are reconciled.

# Part V — Labour, status and bodily cost

## 18. Work changes the body

Costume, makeup and performance must register occupation through:

- calluses;
- cuts and burns;
- dust and fibre;
- salt exposure;
- posture;
- hearing loss or vocal strain;
- repetitive movement;
- sun exposure;
- tool placement;
- repair habits.

No labouring population should look like clean background extras waiting for the principal cast.

## 19. Women’s economic work

The epic makes women visible as performers, trainers, household managers, alms-givers, religious mendicants and providers of sexual or courtly companionship.

### Production rule

The adaptation must avoid two opposite errors:

1. presenting all women’s work as empowered independence;
2. presenting every working woman as without agency.

For each character, the screenplay must ask:

- who controls earnings;
- who selects patrons or clients;
- who owns instruments, clothes and jewellery;
- who bears training costs;
- what protection exists;
- what happens with age, illness or scandal;
- how kinship shapes work.

These answers may differ by household and must be labelled as reconstruction when the text is silent.

## 20. Servitude, dependency and coercion

Royal, elite and commercial households require attendants and workers, but the exact legal status of those people is often unclear.

**[CAUTION]** The film must not casually label every attendant a slave or, conversely, erase coercive dependency by calling everyone a paid employee.

Possible relationships include:

- household service;
- kin-based obligation;
- patronage dependency;
- hired labour;
- bonded or coerced service;
- ritual service;
- apprenticeship.

Each principal household needs a reviewed labour map.

## 21. Poverty is economic exclusion

The hungry in the epic are not a timeless social category. People become vulnerable through failed harvest, illness, injury, widowhood, travel loss, social rejection, imprisonment and loss of work.

The production should connect poverty to disrupted economic roles:

- an unused loom;
- a sold tool;
- an empty boat berth;
- damaged hands;
- a workshop without fuel;
- grain withheld from people without purchasing power;
- jewellery broken apart and sold;
- migration toward relief.

This extends the hunger dossier without turning suffering into spectacle.

# Part VI — Production reconstruction

## 22. Economic districts in Puhar

Puhar’s economy should operate through linked zones rather than a single marketplace:

1. river and sea landing;
2. cargo inspection and temporary storage;
3. wholesale grain and bulk goods;
4. specialist craft lanes;
5. textile and ornament districts;
6. retail and prepared-food spaces;
7. performance and hospitality quarters;
8. royal procurement and treasury access;
9. religious and charitable redistribution;
10. waste, repair and recycling spaces.

Goods must visibly move between these zones.

## 23. Vanji’s inland economy

Vanji should emphasise:

- caravans and pack animals;
- river crossing and storage;
- high-value compact goods;
- metals, stones and forest or upland products;
- court demand;
- religious and intellectual visitors;
- links toward mountain passes and western routes.

It must not look like Puhar without ships.

## 24. Kanchi under famine

The economy dossier connects with 03F by showing what famine interrupts:

- market attendance;
- wages and commissions;
- transport;
- workshop fuel;
- animal health;
- credit and trust;
- availability of buyers;
- state procurement;
- household reserves.

A market can contain goods while many people remain unable to obtain them.

## 25. Soundscape

Economic sound should identify work before exposition:

- hammer and anvil;
- shell sawing;
- bead drilling;
- loom rhythm;
- grain pouring;
- balance weights touching a pan;
- shouted measures;
- animal tack;
- cart wheels;
- harbour rope strain;
- rehearsal percussion;
- market bargaining;
- silence when work collapses.

## 26. Prop traceability

Every hero economic prop must receive:

- object ID;
- material;
- production method;
- source reference;
- owner and user;
- repair history;
- scene continuity;
- confidence level.

This includes coins, weights, balances, seals, tools, jewellery, textile bundles, storage vessels and merchant packages.

---

# Part VII — Binding prohibitions

The production must not:

- present Puhar as a Roman colonial port;
- treat all imported objects as luxury possessions of foreigners;
- use later medieval guild insignia or organisation as early historic fact;
- make every transaction coin-based;
- display decorative pseudo-inscriptions;
- show artisans without tools, waste or unfinished work;
- reduce performers to erotic spectacle;
- romanticise sex work or erase coercion;
- treat merchants as wealth creators independent of labour;
- copy one archaeological workshop as the universal Tamil workshop;
- invent exact taxes, wages, prices or interest rates without evidence;
- equate craft technique with ethnicity.

# Part VIII — Screen opportunities

## 27. Proposed sequence family

### A. Before sunrise: the city prepares

Harbour labour, grain movement, flower workers, musicians and street cleaners begin before elites enter the festival.

### B. The value of a stone

A precious stone passes from courier to merchant to valuer while several workers dispute responsibility for a flaw.

### C. Madhavi’s rehearsal ledger

Training, instrument repair, costume work and patron expectations reveal performance as an economy.

### D. A wreck reaches the market

Salvaged cloth, timber or jewels arrive with missing owners and contested claims, connecting commerce to maritime death.

### E. The mark on the pot

An inscribed vessel becomes evidence in a dispute, but its mark does not provide a simple answer about ownership.

### F. The guild that is not yet medieval

Merchants coordinate a journey through reputation, witnesses and shared risk without later guild banners or military spectacle.

### G. Famine closes the workshop

A skilled household sells tools and stops production before joining the road toward Kanchi’s relief centre.

## Production conclusion

The economy of *Manimekalai* should never be reduced to ships, jewels and crowded bazaars.

Its real cinematic power lies in the chain connecting:

**soil → crop → grain → measure → market → household → hunger → gift**,  
**stone → craft → valuation → ornament → status → sale**,  
**training → performance → patronage → reputation → vulnerability**,  
**voyage → cargo → wreck → loss → redistribution**,  
**labour → wealth → exclusion → ethical responsibility**.

When those chains are visible, the audience can understand why Manimekalai’s decision to feed strangers is not an escape from economic life. It is an intervention within it.
