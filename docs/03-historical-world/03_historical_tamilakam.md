# Historical Tamilakam and the World of the Epic

## Working research edition 0.1

This volume establishes the historical, social, political, religious and material framework for the film. It does **not** treat *Manimekalai* as a transparent historical chronicle. The epic combines remembered places, literary convention, ethical argument, sacred narrative and observations of social life.

## Evidence discipline

- **[TEXT]** Directly supported by *Manimekalai*.
- **[CROSS]** Supported by another early Tamil literary source.
- **[HISTORY]** Supported by archaeology, inscriptions, numismatics or responsible scholarship.
- **[INTERPRETATION]** A cinematic reconstruction where evidence remains incomplete.
- **[CAUTION]** A warning against projecting later assumptions backward.

No visual choice is to be labelled historically exact merely because it is plausible.

## Chronology

The date of the text, the period represented by its narrative and the date of events inherited from *Silappathikaram* are not necessarily identical. The film should avoid an exact modern year until a defensible historical convention is established. The working horizon is early historic Tamilakam, allowing research into early Chola, Pandya and Chera worlds, Indian Ocean trade, Tamil-Brahmi literacy, Buddhist and Jain communities, regional ritual institutions and contact with Sri Lanka and overseas trade networks.

## Political geography

### Puhar

Puhar is treated as a Chola royal port, festival centre, commercial city and meeting place of merchants, artists, ascetics, scholars, officials, warriors and multilingual visitors. It must not resemble a generic later medieval stone-temple city. Its production language should rely on timber, brick, plaster, packed earth, tile, thatch, warehouses, wharves, courtyards, markets, gardens and temporary ritual structures.

### Madurai

Madurai remains psychologically present as the city of Kovalan's execution and Kannagi's demand for justice. The film should reveal this inheritance through memory, testimony, objects and brief flashbacks rather than retelling the whole of *Silappathikaram*.

### Vanji

Vanji should possess a visual identity distinct from maritime Puhar, without relying on later dynastic symbolism. Its reconstruction remains provisional pending literary and archaeological review.

### Kanchi

Kanchi is where philosophy must confront hunger. Intellectual debate should be interwoven with famine, migration, illness, grain scarcity and unequal access to relief.

### Manipallavam

Manipallavam should be presented as both geographical and sacred: a real but liminal maritime place shaped by coral, pale sand, tidal pools, sea birds, wind, relic and silence. Its sacred character should not depend on excessive fantasy effects.

## Puhar as a port city

The city should be organized around three movements:

1. inland produce moving down the Kaveri;
2. maritime goods arriving by sea;
3. people, information, ritual and authority circulating among harbour, market, royal, artistic and religious districts.

Possible harbour activity includes river craft, transfer boats, rope-makers, sail repairers, carpenters, caulkers, porters, scribes, weighers, customs officials, guards, merchants, storage jars, bales and warehouses. Ship designs require a separate evidence dossier and must not be copied wholesale from later reliefs or foreign vessels.

## Archaeological restraint

Archaeology may support the existence and importance of Poompuhar as an early port, but underwater or terrestrial remains must not be promoted as proof of every literary claim, including complete historical verification of a drowned city narrative.

## Urban and social space

For cinematic clarity, Puhar may be mapped into harbour, merchant, artisan, market, performance, elite, royal-administrative, monastic, debate, garden and marginal districts. This is a production reconstruction, not a claim that all boundaries are archaeologically fixed.

Festival streets may show fresh sand, cleaned façades, flowers, leaves, temporary arches, lamps, banners, ritual vessels, sugarcane and banana. Night lighting should remain uneven: wealthy districts glow while marginal areas fall into darkness.

## Indra festival: mandatory caution

The Indra festival in the film must **not** be treated as automatically identical to later pan-Indian, Purāṇic or modern popular representations of Indra worship.

In the epic it functions as a twenty-eight-day royal and civic event connected to rain, prosperity, protection from famine and disease, household welfare, public decoration, royal officers, scholars, religious teachers, philosophers, multilingual visitors and ceremonial military display. The sequence should therefore be staged as a civic-sacral convocation rather than a single standardized temple ritual.

The opening should not require an embodied appearance of Indra. Presence may be communicated through proclamation, song, emblems, weather, public expectation and the city's appeal for order and abundance. The Aputhiran narrative later complicates divine prestige by placing compassionate feeding above heavenly reward.

## Religious plurality

The city contains active disagreement, teaching, debate, persuasion and institutional competition. The screenplay must not describe all non-Buddhist traditions as one already consolidated and uniform religion. It should distinguish civic rites, deity cults, Brahmanical traditions, Buddhism, Jain traditions, ascetic movements, philosophical schools, guardian beings and household practices wherever the evidence permits.

Buddhism must be embodied through institutions, ethical discipline, feeding, causation, renunciation and transregional movement—not reduced to generic meditation imagery.

## Society and gender

Women appear as performer, courtesan, queen, wife, mother, friend, mendicant, goddess, teacher, political actor and renunciant. Terms such as courtesan, dancer, prostitute, temple woman and later *devadasi* must not be treated as interchangeable. Madhavi's artistic and social institution requires a dedicated study.

Social hierarchy should be acknowledged without importing a fully developed later caste structure unchanged into the setting.

## Architecture and costume controls

The production world should use fired and unfired brick, timber, plaster, selected stone foundations, tile, thatch, woven screens, packed earth and painted surfaces. Palaces may be compounds of halls, courtyards, gardens, screens and decorated timber rather than giant stone fortresses.

Costume should communicate region, occupation, wealth, age, climate, ritual position and character transformation. Avoid later Bharatanatyam stage costume, standardized medieval crowns, modern bridal styling and identical robes for every ascetic.

## Food and hunger

Food is a political image. Show grain measured, rice washed, water carried, fires fed, pots blackened and hands waiting. Amudhasurabhi should be dignified and materially believable, its miracle expressed through continuous giving rather than light effects.

Famine must be systemic: empty measures, locked stores, migration, weakened labour, price pressure, livestock loss and abandoned cooking fires.

## Performance and sound

Madhavi's world is a profession and institution, not decorative background. Research must cover training, patronage, gesture, music, audience practice and social vulnerability.

The festival soundscape should layer proclamation drums, merchant calls, ritual recitation, argument, children, animals, wheels, harbour labour, surf and wind rather than rely on uninterrupted orchestral scoring.

## Binding historical visual rules

1. No generic pan-Indian ancient-city design.
2. No dominant later Chola imperial architecture.
3. No later Bharatanatyam costume imposed on Madhavi.
4. No uniform religious population described through modern labels.
5. No automatic equivalence between this festival's Indra and every later depiction.
6. No claim that archaeology proves every literary event.
7. No supernatural effect without thematic purpose.
8. No poverty as anonymous scenery; hunger must have social causes.
9. No philosophical debate detached from character stakes.
10. No modern secular-versus-religious binary imposed on the story.
11. No precise year presented as certain without justification.
12. No single architectural style across all cities.
13. No prop, weapon, textile or ship design without provenance.

## Preliminary Puhar production map

- **River harbour:** grain, timber, labour, measurement and warehouses.
- **Sea harbour:** ships, transfer craft, foreign traders, rope and sail work.
- **Merchant quarter:** luxury goods, credit, interpreters and visiting compounds.
- **Royal-civic quarter:** palace, council space, herald route and festival administration.
- **Performance quarter:** Madhavi's world, rehearsal, musicians and patronage.
- **Sacred-philosophical quarter:** shrines, monasteries, teachers and debate spaces.
- **Gardens:** movement from public desire toward spiritual withdrawal.
- **Marginal Puhar:** casual labour, illness, hunger and people beyond household protection.

## Dramatic thesis

Puhar declares itself prosperous, ordered and protected. Manimekalai's journey reveals those whom its abundance has failed. The tension between proclaimed prosperity and actual compassion is the historical and dramatic foundation of the film.
