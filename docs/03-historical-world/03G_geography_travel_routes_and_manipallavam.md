# 03G — Geography, Travel Routes and Manipallavam

## Production Research Dossier 0.1

**Project:** *Manimekalai* cinematic adaptation  
**Scope:** Puhar, Manipallavam, Nāga country, Vanji, Kanchi, Madurai, northern Sri Lanka, sea passages, inland corridors and travel time  
**Status:** Working research edition. No final production map, route, distance, ancient shoreline or modern-place identification is approved.

---

## 1. Purpose

This dossier establishes a defensible geography for the adaptation without converting literary space into a falsely precise modern atlas.

*Manimekalai* moves through several kinds of space:

- named historical cities;
- sacred islands and memory landscapes;
- maritime regions known through merchants and shipwreck;
- royal territories;
- philosophical centres;
- places inherited from *Silappathikaram*;
- supernatural passages that do not obey ordinary travel time.

These spaces cannot all be mapped with the same degree of certainty. Puhar has a strong modern and archaeological anchor at the Kaveri–sea confluence. Karur has substantial archaeological and literary associations with the Chera world and Vanchi. Northern Sri Lanka contains early historic urban, maritime and Buddhist evidence relevant to the world imagined around Manipallavam and Nāga country. None of this, however, proves that every literary place-name is identical to one excavated modern location.

### Binding methodological rule

> The production atlas will separate textual geography, historical geography and cinematic geography. A modern coordinate may illustrate a hypothesis, but it may not silently convert that hypothesis into fact.

---

## 2. Evidence categories

- **[TEXT]** Directly stated or strongly implied in *Manimekalai*.
- **[CROSS]** Supported by another early Tamil literary source after edition-level verification.
- **[HISTORY]** Supported by archaeology, inscriptions, environmental history or specialist scholarship.
- **[INTERPRETATION]** A controlled production reconstruction required because evidence is incomplete.
- **[CAUTION]** A location, route or identity that must not be presented as settled fact.

### Three maps must always remain distinct

1. **Textual map** — where the epic says characters go and how places relate narratively.
2. **Historical map** — excavated sites, palaeochannels, ports, inscriptions, settlements and defensible route corridors.
3. **Production map** — the coherent spatial system required for sets, travel montage, weather, costume continuity and screen direction.

No production map may be labelled “the map of Manimekalai’s world” without displaying its evidence and uncertainty levels.

# Part I — Fixed and provisional anchors

## 3. Puhar / Kaveripoompattinam

**[TEXT]** Puhar is a Chola royal and maritime city. The story begins within its festival, performance, garden, palace, prison and public-feeding spaces.

**[HISTORY]** The Tamil Nadu Department of Archaeology places Poompuhar at the confluence of the Kaveri and the sea and reports excavation at Kizharveli and Dharmakulam. A brick-and-timber structure at Kizharveli has been interpreted as a wharf of approximately the fourth century CE.

### Production status

Puhar is the project’s strongest geographical anchor. Its exact ancient shoreline, harbour arrangement and urban extent remain uncertain, but the film may confidently treat it as a river–sea port whose life depends on the delta.

### Screen direction

- the sea lies east of the city;
- the Kaveri brings inland goods toward the harbour;
- working districts follow water and cargo movement rather than a rigid grid;
- the horizon and river mouth recur as orientation points;
- weather can alter both harbour labour and civic mood.

## 4. Madurai

Madurai is physically less central to Manimekalai’s present journey than it is emotionally central to her inheritance.

**[TEXT/CROSS]** It is the city associated with Kovalan’s execution, Kannagi’s demand for justice and the destruction that shapes Madhavi and Manimekalai.

**[HISTORY]** Keeladi and other Vaigai-region sites establish a substantial early historic settlement landscape near Madurai. They do not prove individual events from the epics.

### Production status

Madurai should first appear through memory, report and traumatic inheritance. A complete reconstruction of the city belongs to the *Silappathikaram* adaptation archive and should not be improvised solely for flashback spectacle.

## 5. Vanji / Vanchi

**[TEXT]** Vanji is the Chera centre in which Manimekalai enters intellectual communities and hears competing doctrines.

**[HISTORY]** The Tamil Nadu Department of Archaeology identifies Karur in literary and inscriptional traditions as Vanchi and Karuvur. Karur and nearby Pugalur provide Chera dynastic, Jain, trade and early historic evidence. Excavations have produced Tamil-Brahmi sherds, Roman coins, precious stones, rouletted ware, amphora fragments, brick remains and other materials.

### Production status

Karur and the Amaravati river region will serve as the **provisional principal anchor** for Vanji’s production design.

**[CAUTION]** This is not a declaration that every occurrence of Vanchi in every period and literary tradition identifies one unchanging urban footprint. Competing coastal and regional interpretations must be logged during the full concordance review.

### Visual distinction from Puhar

Vanji should feel:

- inland rather than maritime;
- connected to river and overland exchange;
- closer to passes and west–east trade corridors;
- politically compact;
- intellectually crowded;
- materially wealthy without reproducing Puhar’s harbour spectacle.

## 6. Kanchi

**[TEXT]** Kanchi is the city of famine, royal self-questioning, Buddhist instruction, public feeding and the final movement toward disciplined practice.

### Production status

Kanchi is treated as an eastern inland political and religious centre connected to coastal and interior routes. Its early historic city plan, gates, water systems and institutional quarters require a dedicated dossier.

**[CAUTION]** Later Pallava and medieval temple architecture must not become the automatic visual language of Manimekalai’s Kanchi.

# Part II — Manipallavam

## 7. Manipallavam as literary space

**[TEXT]** Manipallavam is reached when the goddess carries the sleeping Manimekalai away from Puhar. It contains sacred features associated with the Buddha, the Gomukhi pond and the appearance of the Amudhasurabhi. It is also connected to previous births, merchant voyages, Nāga rulers and the story of the lost Chola child.

Manipallavam performs several narrative functions:

- refuge from immediate pursuit;
- interruption of ordinary time;
- disclosure of previous lives;
- meeting point of maritime and sacred geography;
- transfer of responsibility through the Amudhasurabhi;
- a place to which human merchants can sail and return.

The final point is essential. Manipallavam is not merely a dream realm. Human ships reach it.

## 8. The Nainativu identification

A longstanding scholarly and popular tradition identifies Manipallavam with Nainativu/Nāgadīpa in the island network west of the Jaffna Peninsula.

**[CAUTION]** The current project has not established this as an archaeological fact.

The identification may be attractive because of:

- the northern Sri Lankan setting associated with Nāga traditions;
- island geography;
- Buddhist sacred traditions connected to Nāgadīpa;
- proximity to maritime routes between Tamilakam and northern Sri Lanka.

It remains insufficient because:

- literary names can shift across time and genre;
- sacred geography may combine more than one remembered place;
- the ancient coastline and island configuration differed from the modern one;
- archaeological evidence from Nainativu does not by itself identify Gomukhi pond or the epic’s complete sacred landscape;
- modern religious and national claims can overstate certainty.

### Binding production decision

Manipallavam will be designed from a **northern Sri Lankan island-zone hypothesis**, not as a labelled reconstruction of modern Nainativu.

Location scouts may study Nainativu and neighbouring islands for:

- low sandy or coral terrain;
- shallow waters;
- lagoon light;
- wind exposure;
- vegetation adapted to salt and limited freshwater;
- boat landing conditions;
- the visual relationship between islands and the Jaffna Peninsula.

No modern shrine, settlement or political boundary will be copied as if it represented the epic’s island.

## 9. Northern Sri Lanka as a historical context

**[HISTORY]** Kantharodai on the Jaffna Peninsula is a major early historic mound site whose early occupation has been dated broadly to the later first millennium BCE. Archaeological studies describe an urban and domestic centre with regional and maritime connections.

**[HISTORY]** Research on the Jaffna Peninsula identifies early ports and trade settlements, including activity around the island of Kayts, with Kantharodai functioning as an inland focus of power and exchange.

**[HISTORY]** Archaeobotanical work at Kantharodai demonstrates rice, millets, pulses and other aspects of settled early historic life.

These findings establish that northern Sri Lanka could sustain urban, agricultural, maritime and religious communities relevant to the epic’s wider world.

**[CAUTION]** They do not prove that Kantharodai, Kayts, Nainativu or any other single site is Manipallavam.

## 10. Manipallavam’s visual system

**[INTERPRETATION]** The island should be physically believable and spiritually altered.

Approved provisional characteristics:

- a low island whose horizon is dominated by sea and sky;
- pale sand, coral or limestone textures used only after geological review;
- sparse shade and wind-shaped vegetation;
- tidal pools and shallow approaches;
- limited freshwater concentrated around a sacred pond or spring system;
- bird calls, surf and wind replacing Puhar’s human density;
- footprints and objects made temporarily vivid by uncluttered ground;
- no giant fantasy ruins;
- no tropical-jungle island stereotype;
- no constant supernatural glow.

Its liminality should arise from editing, memory, sound and altered perception rather than from making the island physically impossible.

# Part III — Nāga country and identity

## 11. Nāga as a literary and historical problem

**[TEXT]** The epic associates Manipallavam and related stories with Nāga rulers, Nāga country, marriage alliances, sacred conflict and maritime movement.

**[CAUTION]** The word Nāga must not be converted automatically into:

- one modern nation;
- one contemporary ethnic group;
- the Naga peoples of Northeast India;
- one race with a fixed costume;
- literal serpent-bodied people in every scene;
- a single archaeological culture.

The term may operate through overlapping registers:

- dynastic or territorial identity;
- clan or lineage memory;
- religious and mythic symbolism;
- island and maritime associations;
- later historiographic interpretation.

### Screen treatment

The Nāga country should be represented first through relationships and institutions:

- rulers and envoys;
- sea knowledge;
- marriage and political kinship;
- island sanctuaries;
- merchant contact;
- regional speech and material culture;
- sacred serpent symbolism only where the text and specialist review support it.

No cast member will be marked as Nāga through racialized makeup or a generic reptile aesthetic.

## 12. Contested modern histories

Northern Sri Lankan archaeological and Buddhist sites carry powerful modern Tamil, Sinhala, Buddhist and national narratives.

**[HISTORY/CAUTION]** Scholarship on Kantharodai shows that a complex multi-ethnic and multi-religious archaeological landscape can be narrowed by modern political claims.

### Production rule

The film must not use archaeology to award exclusive ancient ownership of northern Sri Lanka to a modern community. Consultation must include Tamil, Sinhala and international archaeologists and historians, with disagreements recorded rather than erased.

# Part IV — Maritime corridors

## 13. Puhar to Manipallavam

The epic presents at least two different forms of passage:

1. **supernatural transport** — the goddess carries the sleeping Manimekalai;
2. **human navigation** — merchants travel to the island by ship and can return.

These must not look identical.

### Supernatural passage

- time may contract;
- Manimekalai does not navigate;
- the coast disappears through cloud, sleep, darkness or altered sound;
- the passage should preserve bodily vulnerability rather than resemble superhero flight.

### Merchant passage

- departure depends on provisioning and weather;
- pilots read coast, stars, wind, currents and remembered landmarks;
- vessels may use staged coastal legs or island stops;
- larger ships may remain offshore while smaller boats approach shallow islands;
- arrival is negotiated through surf, reef, sandbar or lagoon conditions.

**[CAUTION]** No straight red line on a modern map proves the actual route.

## 14. Mantai as comparative maritime evidence

**[HISTORY]** Mantai on Sri Lanka’s north-west coast was a major trade and transshipment hub connecting the Arabian Sea, Bay of Bengal, the east and west coasts of India and the island interior from the first millennium BCE onward.

Its shallow channels required differences between large-vessel anchorage and smaller-boat movement. This provides valuable comparative logic for how a northern Sri Lankan maritime zone may operate.

**[CAUTION]** Mantai is not thereby identified as Manipallavam or Nāga country. It is a comparative node demonstrating that the Palk Strait and northern Sri Lankan waters formed active, complex maritime corridors.

## 15. Seasonal navigation

A route is not only a line between places. It is also a decision about when movement is possible.

Production research must eventually record:

- northeast and southwest monsoon effects;
- coastal currents;
- shallow-water hazards;
- visibility and cloud;
- safe beaching conditions;
- water and food capacity;
- sail and hull limitations;
- emergency refuge points;
- different knowledge held by harbour pilots and long-distance navigators.

Until the vessel design and nautical review are complete, no scene may state an exact voyage duration.

# Part V — Overland corridors

## 16. Puhar, Madurai and the river systems

The Kaveri and Vaigai landscapes structure economic and political geography even when Manimekalai does not travel every segment on screen.

**[INTERPRETATION]** Overland movement should follow plausible chains of:

- river valleys;
- market settlements;
- ferries;
- wells and tanks;
- caravan resting places;
- forest edges;
- guarded approaches to royal centres;
- religious establishments offering lodging.

The road must change with ecology rather than appear as one continuous paved highway.

## 17. Puhar to Vanji

Karur’s location on the Amaravati and its connection through the Palghat Gap and east–west exchange make it a strong production anchor for Chera Vanji.

**[HISTORY]** Archaeological route studies place Karur, Kodumanal and coastal or pass-linked settlements within wider trade networks connecting inland production zones and ports.

### Production route hypothesis

Manimekalai’s movement toward Vanji may be staged through:

1. departure from the eastern coastal world;
2. Kaveri-linked inland movement;
3. merchant and agrarian settlements;
4. increasing stone, dryland and upland textures;
5. arrival at the Amaravati corridor;
6. entry into a Chera centre connected to westward passes.

This is a production corridor, not a claim that the epic supplies a complete itinerary.

## 18. Vanji to Kanchi

The journey from Vanji to Kanchi should register a major directional and ecological change.

The production may use:

- dry interior roads;
- river crossings;
- caravan companionship;
- religious lodging networks;
- changing speech and dress;
- reports of famine arriving before the city is visible;
- increasingly disrupted trade as Kanchi’s crisis approaches.

**[CAUTION]** No exact ancient road alignment is approved. Later imperial highways and modern roads may not be projected backward without evidence.

# Part VI — Travel time and cinematic continuity

## 19. Five travel modes

The film will maintain five distinct temporal systems:

### A. Civic walking

Within Puhar, distance is measured through crowds, districts, social exposure and fatigue.

### B. Ordinary overland travel

Measured through days, food, water, rest, dust, weather, companions and changing ecology.

### C. Merchant sea travel

Measured through wind, watch rotations, provisioning, sickness, repairs, landmarks and waiting.

### D. Sacred or supernatural passage

May contract time, but must carry emotional and bodily consequence.

### E. Memory and previous birth

Moves by association rather than geography. It must be visually distinguishable from both ordinary travel and divine transport.

## 20. Distance claims

Ancient units such as *yojana* may appear in translations or commentarial traditions.

### Binding rule

No precise kilometre conversion will appear in dialogue, maps or publicity until:

1. the original Tamil reading is verified;
2. manuscript and edition differences are checked;
3. the narrative use of the number is understood;
4. the variability of the ancient unit is disclosed.

A modern conversion that produces a neat coordinate is not evidence of identification.

## 21. Screen geography continuity

Every travel scene must log:

- origin and destination;
- evidence status of each location;
- direction of travel;
- travel mode;
- elapsed story time;
- season and weather;
- food and water requirements;
- costume wear;
- injuries and fatigue;
- information acquired en route;
- whether the scene is physical, sacred or remembered.

This prevents characters from appearing freshly costumed and emotionally unchanged after difficult travel.

# Part VII — Production atlas

## 22. Atlas confidence levels

Each map feature will use one of four levels:

- **A — Strong anchor:** secure modern-region relationship and substantial evidence.
- **B — Supported region:** broad location supported, exact site unresolved.
- **C — Competing hypotheses:** several plausible modern locations or spatial interpretations.
- **D — Sacred/literary space:** must remain partly non-cartographic.

### Preliminary classifications

| Place | Production confidence | Working treatment |
|---|---:|---|
| Puhar / Kaveripoompattinam | A | Kaveri–sea port zone centred on modern Poompuhar |
| Madurai | A for region; C for epic event sites | Vaigai urban region; memories remain source-controlled |
| Vanji | B/C | Karur–Amaravati as principal production anchor; alternatives logged |
| Kanchi | A for region; C for early city form | Kanchipuram region; architecture and route remain provisional |
| Manipallavam | C/D | Northern Sri Lankan island-zone hypothesis; not fixed to Nainativu |
| Nāga country | C/D | Literary-maritime region without modern ethnic border |
| Mantai | A as archaeological site | Comparative trade node, not a narrative identification |
| Kantharodai | A as archaeological site | Comparative northern urban context, not Manipallavam proof |

## 23. The atlas must show uncertainty

Approved visual devices include:

- solid markers for strong anchors;
- shaded zones for supported regions;
- multiple outlined hypotheses;
- question-marked route corridors;
- separate panels for literary and historical geography;
- palaeoshoreline warnings;
- notes showing that modern coastlines are not ancient coastlines.

The final public map must be more honest than a production whiteboard.

# Part VIII — Scene applications

## 24. The goddess carries Manimekalai

The audience leaves Puhar through fragments:

- festival drums recede;
- harbour lamps become a broken coastline;
- river and sea cease to be distinguishable;
- Manimekalai remains asleep and physically unprotected;
- no visible route establishes a precise destination.

## 25. First morning on Manipallavam

Orientation should be difficult but not impossible. Manimekalai reads:

- direction of sunrise;
- tide marks;
- bird movement;
- salt on vegetation;
- lack of human sound;
- sacred features that do not fit ordinary settlement logic.

The island’s mystery comes from her inability to place herself within known social geography.

## 26. The merchants return

When merchants realise Aputhiran has been left behind, returning to the island must require effort:

- argument over wind and provisions;
- recognition of a navigational error or omission;
- a pilot’s knowledge;
- a cost to the voyage;
- guilt becoming physical action.

## 27. Arrival in Vanji

The film should reveal Vanji through changed exchange systems:

- inland caravans rather than ocean cargo;
- stones, beads, metals and textiles moving through market routes;
- teachers and ascetics arriving from multiple directions;
- river and pass knowledge shaping political power.

## 28. Approach to Kanchi

Famine should become visible along the road before the city gate:

- fewer carts leaving with surplus;
- abandoned or weakened animals;
- travellers seeking information about grain;
- closed lodging places;
- people moving outward;
- guards attempting to control entry or relief.

Geography thus carries the crisis into the story before exposition does.

# Part IX — Prohibited shortcuts

The following are prohibited unless later evidence changes the decision:

1. Labelling modern Nainativu as “the proven Manipallavam.”
2. Equating Nāga country with one modern ethnic or national group.
3. Treating the Naga peoples of Northeast India as the epic’s Nāgas.
4. Using racialised serpent makeup to identify Nāga characters.
5. Presenting modern coastlines as ancient coastlines without warning.
6. Drawing straight-line sea routes as proven navigation tracks.
7. Giving exact voyage times without nautical and textual review.
8. Converting a *yojana* into one fixed modern distance without qualification.
9. Identifying Mantai, Kantharodai, Kayts or Nainativu as Manipallavam solely because each is relevant to northern maritime history.
10. Declaring Karur the only possible Vanji across every text and period.
11. Copying later Pallava or imperial Chola roads, city gates or temples into the epic’s horizon.
12. Making supernatural travel visually identical to ordinary flight.
13. Treating Manipallavam as a generic fantasy island.
14. Using archaeology to support exclusive modern ownership claims in northern Sri Lanka.
15. Allowing travel montage to erase fatigue, provisioning, costume wear or elapsed time.

# Part XI — Production decision record

## GEO-001 — Manipallavam identification

**Decision:** Use a northern Sri Lankan island-zone hypothesis, drawing environmental reference from Nainativu and neighbouring islands, while refusing to label any modern island as proven Manipallavam.

## GEO-002 — Vanji anchor

**Decision:** Use Karur–Amaravati as the principal provisional anchor for Vanji because of Chera, inscriptional and archaeological evidence. Preserve alternate hypotheses in the research apparatus.

## GEO-003 — Nāga representation

**Decision:** Treat Nāga identity as a layered literary, dynastic, maritime and sacred category. Do not map it directly onto a modern ethnicity or use racialised fantasy design.

## GEO-004 — Travel systems

**Decision:** Give walking, overland movement, merchant sailing, divine transport and memory different temporal and visual grammars.

## GEO-005 — Public atlas

**Decision:** Publish uncertainty through confidence levels, zones and competing hypotheses rather than hiding it behind a single clean map.

# 29. Working conclusion

The geography of *Manimekalai* should feel travelled, remembered and sacred—not vaguely exotic.

Puhar is a river–sea city with a strong historical anchor. Vanji can be grounded provisionally in Karur’s Chera and trade landscape. Kanchi and Madurai belong to identifiable regions but require period-controlled urban reconstruction. Northern Sri Lanka demonstrably supported early historic urbanism, ports, farming, Buddhism and long-distance exchange, making it a powerful historical context for Manipallavam and Nāga country.

The adaptation will nevertheless preserve the difference between context and identification.

Manipallavam’s power comes partly from its refusal to become only a dot on a map. It is a place merchants can reach, a place memory can open and a place where Manimekalai accepts responsibility for lives beyond her own. The film must make that island physically convincing while allowing its full meaning to remain larger than geography.
