# 03H — Architecture, Water Systems and Urban Infrastructure

## Production Research Dossier 0.2

**Project:** *Manimekalai* cinematic adaptation  
**Scope:** Puhar, Vanji, Kanchi, Manipallavam support spaces, halls, gardens, prisons, feeding institutions, water systems and civic infrastructure  
**Status:** Working research edition. Literary descriptions, archaeological evidence and production reconstruction are kept separate.

---

## 1. Core rule

Ancient cities in *Manimekalai* are not reconstructed from one surviving building type. The adaptation separates:

1. **Textual architecture** — places named or described in the epic.
2. **Archaeological architecture** — excavated walls, wells, wharves, bricks, reservoirs and comparable structures.
3. **Cinematic architecture** — coherent environments created for storytelling.

A poetic description of splendour is not a construction drawing.

### Binding methodological rule

> No surviving structure, water feature or later monument may silently become the exact building described in the epic.

---

# Part I — Puhar, the maritime city

## 2. A city of linked functions

**[TEXT]** Puhar contains festival streets, gardens, performance spaces, temples, public halls, royal spaces, feeding locations, warehouses implied by its maritime economy and places of confinement.

The production should not treat these as isolated sets. Their relationships create the city:

- river and harbour activity supplies markets;
- markets supply households and festivals;
- households supply alms;
- streets connect performance, ritual and political power;
- confinement and public care reveal competing uses of state space.

## 3. Wharf and water-port evidence

**[HISTORY]** The Tamil Nadu Department of Archaeology reports excavation at Kizharveli and Dharmakulam in Poompuhar. Brick walls, platforms and wooden posts at Kizharveli have been interpreted as a wharf. Marine and onshore research also reports ring wells, bricks, wooden material and remains from multiple periods. **Sources: SRC-0005, SRC-0006, SRC-0010.**

### Production reconstruction

Puhar should appear as a distributed water-dependent port containing:

- river landing areas;
- temporary cargo platforms;
- warehouses close to movement routes;
- repair and recycling spaces;
- artisan districts;
- performance and festival zones;
- socially differentiated residential streets;
- public spaces shaped by shade, water and crowd movement.

The harbour remains a system, not a single monumental stone quay.

---

# Part II — Building technologies

## 4. Brick, timber, plaster and roofing

**[HISTORY]** Keeladi provides comparative early historic evidence for brick structures, post holes, iron nails and grooved roofing tiles tied to timber rafters. Similar roof tiles are reported from other early historic southern sites. **Sources: SRC-0038, SRC-0041.**

### Production principle

The city should display mixed construction:

- fired and unfired brick where justified;
- timber posts, beams and rafters;
- plaster and surface renewal;
- tile and thatch serving different buildings;
- courtyards and shaded transitional space;
- repair patches and replacement materials.

Stone may appear where evidence and function support it, but it must not dominate the city merely because stone survives archaeologically better than wood.

## 5. Poetic materials

The epic’s references to gold, marble, shining halls and extraordinary beauty may signal splendour, sacred perception, wealth or poetic convention.

**[CAUTION]** They do not automatically prove:

- solid gold construction;
- modern polished marble walls;
- enormous unsupported spans;
- later palace plans;
- fantasy materials without weathering or maintenance.

Every spectacular material requires a literal, symbolic or cinematic classification.

# Part III — Water systems

## 6. Water is infrastructure

Water must be designed as a working urban system rather than decorative scenery.

Potential components include:

- household and neighbourhood wells;
- ring wells;
- ponds and tanks;
- river access points;
- storage jars and basins;
- roof drainage;
- open or covered surface channels where evidenced;
- washing and production water;
- waste and soakage management;
- emergency water for public feeding.

## 7. Ring-well caution

**[HISTORY]** Ring wells have been excavated at Keeladi, Kanchipuram-region sites, Vasavasamudram and other early historic settlements. At Vasavasamudram, ring wells occurred near a brick-lined tank tentatively associated with washing or dyeing. A deep ring well and early historic material are reported at Irulanthoppu. **Sources: SRC-0039, SRC-0040, SRC-0041.**

### Binding rule

A ring well cannot automatically be labelled:

- a drinking-water well;
- a sewer;
- a soakage pit;
- a toilet feature;
- an industrial installation.

Its function depends on depth, deposits, associated structures, hydrology and specialist interpretation.

## 8. Water and inequality

The same city may contain:

- elite garden water;
- household water carried by labourers;
- harbour water unsuitable for drinking;
- public wells under crowd pressure;
- religious ponds;
- water withheld from prisoners;
- failing access during famine.

Water access should reveal class, gender, occupation and institutional priority without inventing a modern municipal network.

# Part IV — Institutions

## 9. Public feeding architecture

The Amudhasurabhi scenes require architecture of care.

A functioning feeding space should include:

- accessible approach routes;
- water availability;
- preparation and serving zones;
- storage and cleaning;
- shade and rest;
- routes for people with limited mobility;
- separation of animal feeding where public health requires it;
- waste and vessel return;
- crowd coordination without humiliation.

The miracle does not replace labour, organisation or dignity.

## 10. Prison transformation

**[TEXT]** The king changes the prison into a charitable institution with feeding, kitchens and lodging.

The adaptation must show transformation of purpose.

### Before

- controlled entrances;
- guarded circulation;
- restricted water and food;
- cells or holding spaces;
- surveillance and punishment.

### After

- opened circulation where appropriate;
- kitchens and water points;
- sleeping and recovery space;
- food storage;
- service and care roles;
- revised legal and administrative control.

A new institution is created, not merely a decorated prison.

## 11. Royal spaces

Royal architecture should communicate access and procedure rather than later imperial monumentality.

The production needs distinct spaces for:

- petition and judgement;
- private family grief;
- guarded confinement;
- consultation with ministers and teachers;
- storage and procurement;
- public proclamation;
- transition between palace and city.

No exact throne hall, palace height or fortification system is approved without further evidence.

---

# Part V — Boundary and sacred spaces

## 12. Chakravala and the cremation-ground landscape

**[TEXT]** Chakravala is a boundary landscape associated with walls, entrances, death, refuge, ascetics and philosophical reflection.

It must not become a horror-fantasy cemetery.

Its design should use:

- controlled thresholds;
- traces of funerary work;
- ash, fire and earth without sensational gore;
- social distance from ordinary streets;
- shelters and ritual structures where supported;
- sound and negative space;
- the visible coexistence of fear, exclusion and contemplation.

## 13. Manipallavam support spaces

Manipallavam is not a ruined fantasy metropolis. Its built environment should remain sparse and subordinate to island, pond, sacred seat and memory.

Possible elements include:

- boat landing traces;
- a modest shelter or custodial space;
- paths shaped by repeated ritual movement;
- water management around Gomukhi pond;
- sacred surfaces whose age and maintenance are visible.

No modern shrine plan may be copied as the epic’s exact island architecture.

---

# Part VI — Vanji and Kanchi

## 14. Vanji

Vanji should differ from Puhar through:

- inland river connections;
- caravan and storage infrastructure;
- administrative spaces;
- compact teaching environments;
- trade routes rather than harbour spectacle;
- brick, timber and local material combinations distinct from the coast.

Karur remains a provisional production anchor rather than an absolute identification.

## 15. Kanchi

Kanchi requires strict chronology because its surviving identity is dominated by later monuments.

### Binding rule

Later Pallava and imperial Chola architecture must not automatically represent *Manimekalai*’s earlier world.

The film should prioritise:

- famine-affected streets;
- wells, storage and failing access;
- royal decision spaces;
- teaching spaces;
- relief infrastructure;
- public feeding;
- links between inland city and coastal trade routes.

Early historic ring-well, pottery, roofing and writing evidence may guide material systems without supplying a complete city plan. **Source: SRC-0039.**

---

# Part VII — Production prohibitions

Do not use:

- later temple towers as default ancient scenery;
- medieval palace assumptions;
- fantasy stone megacities;
- modern urban planning grids;
- unsupported sewer, aqueduct or harbour claims;
- one architectural language for Puhar, Vanji and Kanchi;
- clean sets without maintenance, waste or service routes;
- monumental scale as the only expression of power;
- archaeological features detached from their context.

## Architecture principle

The city of *Manimekalai* is defined less by isolated monuments than by relationships:

**water connects labour,**  
**food connects ethics,**  
**streets connect communities,**  
**thresholds distribute power,**  
**and institutions reveal political choices.**
