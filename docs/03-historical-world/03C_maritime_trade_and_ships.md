# 03C — Maritime Trade and Ships

## Production Research Dossier 0.1

**Project:** *Manimekalai* cinematic adaptation  
**Scope:** Puhar harbour, merchant networks, vessels, crews, cargo, navigation, shipwreck and screen reconstruction  
**Status:** Working research edition. No vessel design is approved for construction or final concept art.

---

## 1. Purpose

This dossier establishes a defensible maritime world for the adaptation without turning partial evidence into false certainty.

The film requires ships because the epic’s moral and geographical world is maritime. Puhar exists at a river–sea junction; Manipallavam is reached across water; merchants move between countries; storms destroy vessels; survivors reach unfamiliar shores; goods recovered from wrecks include valuable woods, jewels and cloth; and the sea links Puhar to the Nāga country and other regions.

The goal is therefore not to design one spectacular “ancient Tamil ship” from imagination. The goal is to create a **coherent family of working vessels and harbour practices**, with every element labelled according to the strength of its evidence.

---

## 2. Evidence categories

- **[TEXT]** Directly present in *Manimekalai*.
- **[CROSS]** Supported by another early Tamil literary source.
- **[HISTORY]** Supported by archaeology, numismatics, epigraphy or specialist maritime scholarship.
- **[INTERPRETATION]** A production reconstruction required because evidence is incomplete.
- **[CAUTION]** A claim or visual convention that must not be presented as established fact.

### Binding methodological rule

> A vessel type known generally from the Indian Ocean is not automatically proven for Puhar, and an imported object found in Tamilakam does not automatically prove that a foreign-built ship docked at Puhar.

# Part I — The maritime world inside *Manimekalai*

## 3. Puhar is more than a coastal backdrop

**[TEXT]** The epic presents Puhar as a large royal and commercial city whose population includes merchants, visitors and speakers of different languages. The civic world of the Indra festival assumes movement of people and goods beyond the immediate city.

**[INTERPRETATION]** The harbour is therefore not an isolated establishing shot. It must remain visible in the life of the city:

- tides alter the working day;
- ships determine when labour is hired;
- warehouses shape street movement;
- foreign news travels through crews and brokers;
- elite consumption depends on distant cargo;
- hunger exists beside commercial wealth;
- storms can erase fortunes and families.

## 4. Sāduvan’s voyage and shipwreck

**[TEXT]** In Chapter 16, Sāduvan explains that he travelled on a ship, encountered a storm, was separated from the people aboard and reached an island after suffering in the wave-filled ocean.

This passage establishes several narrative facts:

1. merchants undertake sea voyages;
2. storms can break or scatter voyages;
3. passengers and crew may believe someone has died or been lost;
4. survivors may reach foreign shores;
5. communication across language boundaries is possible;
6. shipwreck is a recurring risk rather than a singular mythic event.

**[TEXT]** Sāduvan advises the island community to rescue people who reach shore after ships break and sink, rather than killing survivors or taking advantage of them.

### Dramatic importance

The shipwreck story is not merely an adventure episode. It tests civilisation through its treatment of the vulnerable. Maritime danger becomes an ethical question:

> What does a society owe a stranger who has lost ship, crew, wealth and protection?

## 5. Wreck goods and cargo categories

**[TEXT]** Goods associated with sailors and wrecks in the Sāduvan narrative include fragrant sandalwood, precious jewels and soft cloth.

These items support a high-value cargo economy, but they do not provide a complete manifest for every ship.

### Production use

Approved preliminary cargo categories are:

- bundled or stacked textiles;
- protected jewel containers;
- aromatic wood or timber pieces;
- food and water stores for the voyage;
- rope, spare sail material and repair timber;
- ceramics and baskets for practical storage;
- merchant-owned sealed packages.

**[CAUTION]** Amphorae, pepper sacks, wine jars, gold coin chests or exotic animals must not be placed prominently on a Puhar ship merely because they occur somewhere in Indian Ocean trade. Each hero prop requires a source note.

## 6. Merchant voyages to Manipallavam

**[TEXT]** Chapter 25 remembers nine merchant companions travelling by ship with Āputhiran and returning after realizing that they had abandoned him on the island.

This implies:

- organised merchant groups;
- repeated or reversible voyages;
- enough navigational knowledge to return to an island;
- collective responsibility within a trading party;
- ships carrying merchants as active participants, not merely passive cargo owners.

**[INTERPRETATION]** The film may distinguish between the vessel’s professional crew and the merchant group travelling with its goods. The text does not require every merchant to be a sailor.

## 7. Nāga country and maritime geography

**[TEXT]** The epic connects Puhar, Manipallavam and the Nāga country through travel, kinship and memory. The precise historical geography of these names requires a separate dossier.

**[CAUTION]** The adaptation must not prematurely identify every Nāga location with one modern nation, ethnic community or archaeological site.

### Screen principle

The sea is not an empty gap between story locations. It is a travelled cultural zone containing:

- routes known to pilots;
- islands and seasonal stopping places;
- dangers remembered by crews;
- multilingual encounters;
- sacred geography;
- political and commercial relationships.

# Part II — Cross-textual and external historical context

## 8. *Pattinappālai* and the port tradition

**[CROSS — PROVISIONAL]** *Pattinappālai* is an essential source for the commercial and urban imagination of Puhar. Published scholarship commonly reads the poem as describing large vessels, imported merchandise, merchants and an active port economy.

Before final screenplay locking, the project must add:

- the original Tamil passage;
- a literal project translation;
- comparison of at least two scholarly translations;
- philological notes on vessel, harbour, cargo and merchant terms;
- a record of where translation choices are disputed.

**[CAUTION]** A modern English phrase such as “ships enter without lowering their sails” must not be treated as a technical naval description until the Tamil wording and commentarial tradition are reviewed.

## 9. *Silappathikaram*

**[CROSS — PENDING]** *Silappathikaram* provides the most important narrative and urban cross-context for Puhar, Madhavi, Kovalan and the cultural world inherited by *Manimekalai*.

Its maritime references must be extracted into a separate concordance covering:

- harbour districts;
- merchant communities;
- foreign goods;
- coastal movement;
- festivals and civic order;
- the relationship between wealth and performance culture.

No production detail should be attributed to *Silappathikaram* without chapter- and line-level verification.

## 10. The *Periplus of the Erythraean Sea*

**[HISTORY]** The *Periplus* is a Greek-language guide to routes, ports and trade in the Red Sea and Indian Ocean, usually dated to the first century CE. It demonstrates that merchants used seasonal wind knowledge to connect Egyptian Red Sea ports, Arabia and Indian markets.

### Proper use in this project

The *Periplus* can support the broader reality of:

- organised long-distance maritime commerce;
- route knowledge;
- seasonal sailing;
- specialised ports and markets;
- cargo planning;
- commercial intelligence transmitted among merchants.

### Improper use

It must not be used to claim automatically that:

- the anonymous author visited Puhar;
- every vessel in Puhar followed a Red Sea design;
- Roman sailors personally crewed all ships carrying Mediterranean goods to Tamilakam;
- western Indian Ocean routes and Bay of Bengal routes used identical ships or practices.

## 11. Comparative Tamil coastal archaeology

**[HISTORY]** Excavations at Alagankulam have yielded Mediterranean-region pottery, including amphora fragments and rouletted ware, Tamil-Brahmi-inscribed red ware, beads, structural material and Roman coins from different chronological contexts.

This supports participation of the Tamil coast in long-distance exchange networks.

**[CAUTION]** Alagankulam evidence is comparative. It cannot be transferred item-for-item to Puhar. Imported ceramics may have moved through several ports and intermediaries.

# Part III — Archaeological foundation at Poompuhar

## 12. River–sea location

**[HISTORY]** The Tamil Nadu Department of Archaeology identifies Poompuhar at the confluence of the Kaveri and the sea and describes it as a flourishing Sangam-age Chola port.

### Production consequence

The harbour must be designed around a shifting delta rather than as a perfectly engineered, permanent stone basin.

The production model should include:

- river channels whose depth changes seasonally;
- sandbars and shoals;
- tidal movement;
- erosion and deposition;
- different working areas for river craft and ocean-going vessels;
- structures repeatedly repaired or rebuilt.

## 13. Kizharveli wharf interpretation

**[HISTORY]** The Department reports two brick walls, platforms and wooden poles at Kizharveli. The space between the walls permitted water movement, and the structure has been interpreted as a wharf dating approximately to the fourth century CE. Palmyra and iluppai wood are reported among the poles.

### What may be used on screen

- brick-and-timber waterside infrastructure;
- platforms allowing water movement;
- wooden posts used for working or securing river craft;
- constant wetness, silt and maintenance;
- labourers operating close to water level.

### What remains uncertain

- the complete original elevation;
- whether every component served vessel berthing;
- the precise relationship to the epic’s literary period;
- the size of vessels directly using the structure;
- the appearance of cranes, capstans or other lifting devices.

## 14. Underwater structural evidence

**[HISTORY]** Government summaries report offshore exploration, stone masonry near Vanagiri and features tentatively dated between the second century BCE and fourth century CE.

**[CAUTION]** The remains do not provide a complete harbour plan. Their purpose, chronology and relationship to literary Puhar require specialist review of full reports rather than reliance on short website descriptions.

## 15. The later shipwreck problem

**[HISTORY]** A wreck explored off Poompuhar contained a cannon and lead ingots marked with dates in the 1790s. It is an early modern wreck, not evidence for vessels in the *Manimekalai* period.

### Binding production rule

> The eighteenth-century wreck must never be used as the model for an ancient Puhar merchant ship.

It may be relevant to the long maritime history of the coast, but not to the film’s hull reconstruction.

# Part IV — Harbour reconstruction for the film

## 16. Working harbour system

The production will not build one universal dock. Puhar will use a **distributed harbour system**.

### Zone 1 — River landing

**Primary use:** grain, firewood, timber, local produce and passengers arriving from inland.

Screen elements:

- shallow-draft craft;
- poles and paddles;
- ramps or muddy landings;
- baskets and grain containers;
- animals and carts waiting beyond the wet edge;
- scribes or weighers in nearby shelters.

### Zone 2 — Wharf and transfer area

**Primary use:** loading, unloading and transferring goods between craft.

Screen elements:

- brick platforms;
- timber posts;
- temporary planks;
- ropes darkened by water;
- workers moving in coordinated teams;
- repair work continuing beside commerce.

### Zone 3 — Estuary anchorage

**Primary use:** medium and larger craft awaiting tide, pilot or cargo.

Screen elements:

- vessels turning with tide and wind;
- anchors or mooring systems kept visually plausible but not over-specified;
- smaller boats approaching larger hulls;
- pilots judging channels;
- signal calls between shore and vessel.

### Zone 4 — Offshore roadstead

**[INTERPRETATION]** Larger sea-going vessels may remain offshore or in deeper water and use smaller transfer boats when channel depth is inadequate.

This is a production solution consistent with a dynamic river mouth, but its exact historical application to Puhar remains provisional.

### Zone 5 — Warehouse belt

The warehouse district should contain:

- ventilated storage;
- raised or protected goods;
- controlled entrances;
- weighing areas;
- merchant marks;
- guards;
- fire risk management;
- water jars and food for labourers;
- customs or royal oversight without modern office architecture.

# Part V — Vessel family for production design

## 17. Why one “hero ship” is insufficient

A credible port contains vessels built for different waters, loads and distances. The project will develop at least four classes before final screenplay storyboarding.

## 18. Class R1 — River and delta craft

**Function:** movement through the Kaveri channels and shallow harbour margins.

Possible features:

- shallow draft;
- open cargo space;
- paddle or pole propulsion;
- modest sail only if supported by the final design study;
- capacity for baskets, grain, people and animals.

**[CAUTION]** No exact length is approved.

## 19. Class H1 — Harbour transfer boat

**Function:** carry passengers and cargo between shore and anchored vessels.

Possible features:

- broad working floor;
- reinforced rubbing areas;
- several rowers or paddlers;
- removable covers for valuable cargo;
- rope fenders made from locally defensible materials.

This class is essential for visualising a port without requiring every large ship to touch the quay.

## 20. Class C1 — Coastal merchant vessel

**Function:** movement between ports along the Coromandel coast and nearby islands.

Possible features:

- higher sides than river craft;
- protected cargo area;
- mast and woven sail;
- steering system suited to coastal sailing;
- space for merchants, provisions and a small crew;
- repair materials carried aboard.

**[INTERPRETATION]** The ship should look capable of beaching or using imperfect anchorages, but this must be reviewed by a nautical archaeologist.

## 21. Class O1 — Ocean-going merchant vessel

**Function:** longer voyages across the Bay of Bengal or connected Indian Ocean routes.

Required visual qualities:

- visibly stronger hull and internal structure;
- greater freeboard;
- larger water and food capacity;
- sleeping and shelter arrangements that are uncomfortable but practical;
- cargo protected from spray and shifting;
- crew roles legible through action;
- repair capacity for extended voyage.

### Prohibited assumptions

The O1 vessel must not yet be labelled as:

- a specific named ancient Tamil ship type;
- a Roman vessel;
- a later Arab dhow;
- a medieval Chola naval ship;
- an exact Ajanta reconstruction;
- a modern Kerala cargo boat moved backward in time.

## 22. Class P1 — Elite or royal transport

If the screenplay requires elite maritime travel, it should be based on a practical vessel modified through:

- cleaner finish;
- awning;
- guards;
- controlled seating;
- symbolic textiles or standards.

It must not become a floating palace unsupported by evidence.

# Part VI — Hull construction and rig

## 23. Sewn-plank construction

**[HISTORY]** Sewn-plank vessels are widely documented in Indian Ocean maritime scholarship across long periods. Planks are joined using fibre stitching or lashing systems rather than relying only on metal fasteners.

### Production position

Sewn-plank construction is a **serious candidate** for one or more production vessel classes, but not yet a proven Puhar-specific fact.

### Required review before adoption

- chronological fit;
- regional construction traditions;
- plank edge treatment;
- stitching pattern;
- internal framing sequence;
- fibre type;
- caulking material;
- hull flexibility;
- maintenance requirements.

**[CAUTION]** A modern stitched-ship reconstruction inspired by a later painting can demonstrate engineering possibility, but cannot by itself establish the exact appearance of a vessel in *Manimekalai*.

## 24. Timber

The wharf evidence reports palmyra and iluppai poles, but that does not prove that ship hulls were built from those species.

The shipbuilding timber dossier must examine:

- availability;
- plank dimensions;
- bending qualities;
- resistance to marine organisms;
- repairability;
- literary names;
- archaeological survival bias.

Until then, concept art must not caption a hull with a specific timber species.

## 25. Sail plan

The project currently approves only the following broad statement:

> Sea-going vessels used wind power, but the exact sail geometry and rig for Puhar ships remain unresolved.

Concept artists may explore a limited number of alternatives, clearly marked as hypotheses.

### Avoid

- automatically using a modern triangular lateen sail;
- copying a Mediterranean square-rigged merchantman;
- reproducing a medieval dhow;
- creating multiple masts for scale without evidence;
- designing decorative sails that could not function.

## 26. Steering

No final steering system is approved.

Research alternatives may include steering oars or quarter rudders appropriate to the broader maritime context. A sternpost rudder must not be introduced without chronological and regional support.

# Part VII — Crew, labour and commercial organisation

## 27. Vessel roles

The film should distinguish at least the following functions, although historical terminology remains to be researched:

- vessel master;
- pilot familiar with coast, channel and season;
- helmsman or steering crew;
- sail and rigging crew;
- rowers or paddlers where appropriate;
- carpenter or repair specialist;
- merchants accompanying cargo;
- guards on valuable consignments;
- cook or person responsible for water and provisions;
- interpreter or multilingual broker on some voyages.

Not every ship requires a modern hierarchical uniform. Roles should be revealed through work.

## 28. Dock labour

The harbour must show specialised cooperation:

- unloading teams;
- weighers;
- tally keepers;
- rope handlers;
- warehouse workers;
- guards;
- brokers;
- porters;
- boatmen;
- craftspeople repairing sails, baskets and containers;
- food sellers serving workers.

### Social principle

Harbour labour should not be anonymous spectacle. Recurring background characters can reveal:

- injury;
- debt;
- seasonal employment;
- dependence on merchant arrivals;
- exclusion from the wealth they move.

This supports the epic’s larger concern with hunger inside a prosperous city.

# Part VIII — Navigation and voyage conditions

## 29. Seasonal knowledge

**[HISTORY]** Indian Ocean commerce depended on knowledge of seasonal winds. The *Periplus* records merchants planning voyages around monsoon conditions.

**[INTERPRETATION]** Puhar sailors may be shown discussing season, current, cloud, river discharge and departure windows without using modern meteorological explanation.

## 30. Coastal navigation

Plausible navigation practices for research and visual development include:

- coastal landmarks;
- river colour and sediment;
- stars;
- sun direction;
- swell and current;
- birds;
- water depth measured by line;
- inherited route knowledge;
- pilots recruited for unfamiliar approaches.

**[CAUTION]** No specific navigational instrument should receive a hero close-up until its period and regional use are verified.

## 31. Life aboard

A long voyage must feel physically difficult:

- restricted fresh water;
- salted or dried food;
- heat below covers;
- wet clothing;
- insects and vermin;
- cargo shifting;
- seasickness;
- sleep interrupted by watches;
- constant rope, sail and hull maintenance;
- fear of losing landfall.

The film should resist romanticising every voyage as liberation or discovery.

# Part IX — Shipwreck sequence design

## 32. Sāduvan storm: narrative objectives

The sequence must achieve four things:

1. establish the skill and vulnerability of maritime travellers;
2. make separation from the vessel physically understandable;
3. avoid modern disaster-film exaggeration;
4. prepare the ethical encounter with the island community.

## 33. Proposed sequence structure

### Beat 1 — Weather changes before spectacle

- altered swell;
- crew attention shifts;
- cargo is secured;
- sail is reduced or managed according to final rig design;
- merchants are ordered away from working areas;
- experienced crew understand the risk before passengers do.

### Beat 2 — Loss of control

- vessel heels or yaws;
- water enters;
- lashings fail or cargo moves;
- visibility collapses;
- shouted instructions become difficult to hear.

### Beat 3 — Separation

Sāduvan’s loss must be staged according to the final textual reading. He may be washed away, left with wreckage, or separated during evacuation, but the screenplay must not invent a precise mechanism until the Tamil passage is checked.

### Beat 4 — Ocean aftermath

- no heroic music;
- breath, water and distant debris;
- uncertain time;
- loss of social identity;
- arrival on an unfamiliar shore.

## 34. Safety and VFX policy

The sequence should combine:

- controlled full-scale deck section;
- tank work;
- practical rain and water;
- stunt doubles;
- digital ocean extension;
- miniature or digital hull only where necessary.

The ship must behave consistently with the approved naval architecture model. It cannot change dimensions or rig between wide shots and close work.

# Part X — Screen opportunities in Puhar

## 35. Opening harbour sequence

### Visual progression

1. darkness and river sound;
2. fishers and boatmen moving before sunrise;
3. inland craft arriving with produce;
4. larger vessels visible beyond the river channel;
5. transfer boats crossing between hull and shore;
6. warehouse doors opening;
7. royal or customs oversight beginning;
8. festival decorations transforming the same working streets.

### Thematic purpose

The sequence establishes abundance before the film reveals who remains hungry.

## 36. Manimekalai’s route through the city

When Manimekalai walks through Puhar, maritime trade should enter the frame through ordinary details:

- sailors buying flowers or food;
- a merchant dictating a letter;
- wet cargo cloth drying;
- foreign words heard briefly;
- a porter with an injured shoulder;
- children gathering spilled grain;
- a widow waiting for news of a ship;
- wealth carried past people who cannot eat.

## 37. Departure for Manipallavam

A departure scene must show preparation rather than instant travel:

- water and food counted;
- route and weather debated;
- cargo balanced;
- offerings or protective rites appropriate to the researched community;
- family farewell;
- pilot boarding;
- vessel leaving river, coast and finally land behind.

# Part XI — Visual rules

## 38. Binding rules for concept art

1. Do not design one universal vessel for river, harbour, coast and ocean.
2. Do not use the eighteenth-century Poompuhar wreck as ancient evidence.
3. Do not place Roman military or merchant ships at Puhar without direct justification.
4. Do not copy medieval Chola naval imagery into the early historic setting.
5. Do not label sewn-plank construction as certain for Puhar before specialist review.
6. Do not use modern dhow silhouettes as a shortcut for “Indian Ocean.”
7. Do not overcrowd every ship with decorative carving.
8. Do not make all sails new, clean and identical.
9. Do not make harbour water clear and blue; show silt, tide, waste and labour.
10. Do not make cargo visually random. Every featured item requires provenance.
11. Do not portray merchants as exclusively foreign or exclusively elite.
12. Do not let spectacle erase dock labour or maritime risk.

## 39. Colour and surface

Maritime surfaces should be practical and used:

- salt-whitened rope;
- darkened working timber;
- patched sailcloth;
- wet brick;
- clay, silt and sand;
- protective oils or resins only where technically plausible;
- faded merchant markings;
- textiles protected from spray rather than displayed like a market stall.

## 40. Soundscape

The harbour score should leave space for:

- water against hulls;
- rope strain;
- wood movement;
- paddle rhythm;
- shouted distances;
- cargo impacts;
- birds;
- sailcloth;
- tools;
- multilingual bargaining;
- festival drums gradually overtaking the working harbour.

# Part XII — Approved provisional production decisions

## 41. Distributed harbour model

**Decision:** Puhar will be presented as river landing, wharf, estuary anchorage, offshore roadstead and warehouse belt rather than one monumental enclosed harbour.

**Status:** Provisional; compatible with current evidence and delta conditions.

## 42. Vessel family

**Decision:** Production design will develop separate river, transfer, coastal and ocean-going vessels.

**Status:** Binding provisional.

## 43. Ocean-going hull uncertainty

**Decision:** No exact Puhar ocean-going hull, sail plan or steering system will be declared historically certain in publicity or production notes until specialist review.

**Status:** Binding.

## 44. Sewn-plank candidate

**Decision:** Sewn-plank construction may be explored in concept design but must remain labelled a comparative Indian Ocean hypothesis.

**Status:** Provisional.

## 45. Foreign trade representation

**Decision:** Show Tamilakam as connected to long-distance trade without requiring foreign-built ships to appear in Puhar. Goods, people and information may move through intermediaries.

**Status:** Binding provisional.

# 50. Working conclusion

The adaptation should present Puhar as a city that understands the sea but never controls it completely.

Its people possess accumulated knowledge of river, tide, coast, wind, cargo and route. Their ships make wealth, migration, kinship and religious exchange possible. Yet a storm can strip a merchant of property, language, protection and status in a single night.

That tension is the heart of the maritime design:

> The sea creates Puhar’s prosperity, and the sea exposes how fragile prosperity is.
