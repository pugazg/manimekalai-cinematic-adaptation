# 10D — Act I Treatment

## The City That Desires

**Runtime:** 45 minutes  
**Sequences:** `SEQ-01` to `SEQ-03`  
**Units:** 1–17

---

# SEQ-01 — The City Before the Storm

Puhar wakes before sunrise.

Water carriers move between wells, tanks and household thresholds. Harbour workers unload grain and timber while sailors repair rope and inspect hull seams. Flower workers sort garlands for the Indra festival. Musicians tune instruments in courtyards not yet opened to patrons. Cleaners remove refuse from ceremonial routes. Animal keepers feed and settle creatures that will later appear as signs of civic abundance. The city’s wealth is visible, but so is the labour that makes wealth appear effortless.

A proclamation drum begins its route. The festival is announced as a promise of order, rainfall and prosperity. The words reach merchants, artisans, religious communities and royal officers differently. Some hear celebration; others hear obligation. The sceptre and the rain are linked publicly: rule is expected to protect life, not merely sponsor spectacle.

The drum reaches Madhavi’s quarter and encounters silence.

The former performance house is not abandoned, but its rhythms have changed. Rehearsal rooms have become restrained working spaces. Instruments remain cared for, proving that artistic knowledge has not been rejected as contamination. Yet the doors do not open for festival expectation. Workers and dependants are uncertain about income, status and their own place in the altered household.

A brief memory enters through Madhavi rather than interrupting the film as an independent prologue. After Kovalan’s death, she brings grief she cannot organise into words to Aravana Adigal. He does not explain it away or demand repentance. He listens. The memory ends before doctrine begins. In the present, the audience understands that Madhavi’s renunciation is not theatrical purity; it is a response to loss, responsibility and fear.

Inside the household, Chitrapathi, Madhavi and Manimekalai argue about the younger woman’s future. Chitrapathi speaks from the material intelligence of a performance lineage: training, reputation, patronage and the survival of dependants. Madhavi speaks from grief and the desire to prevent her daughter from being claimed by the same social world that consumed her own choices. Both women describe protection, but each risks turning protection into authorship over Manimekalai.

Manimekalai listens before speaking. She is not presented as already enlightened. She is angry that every available future is explained through someone else’s history—lineage, livelihood, fame, renunciation, danger or maternal repair. She rejects the idea that she must become either Madhavi’s continuation or Madhavi’s correction. Her first clear act is not a religious declaration but a boundary: her life is not a second life granted to the older women.

Outside, the festival city gathers multiple communities beneath a shared civic order. Teachers and adherents speak in halls, shaded courtyards and temporary debate spaces. Competing claims about authority, causation and liberation are not yet explained in full. The sequence simply establishes that Puhar contains many paths and that no institution possesses the entire intellectual city.

The sequence closes on a contrast. The proclamation continues through streets brightening toward celebration, while Manimekalai stands inside a house whose future has become uncertain because she has spoken for herself.

**Sequence exit:** Manimekalai’s autonomy is explicit, but she still lives inside a city structured to interpret her before it hears her.

# SEQ-02 — The Woman Who Refuses a Destiny

Manimekalai and Sudhamathi leave the household to gather flowers.

The route is not treated as neutral. Sudhamathi chooses the safer side of streets, reads crowd density, notices who is watching and avoids spaces where rank can close exits. Her vigilance is practical rather than mystical. She and Manimekalai move as friends who can disagree, tease and correct each other.

In a protected garden space, Sudhamathi explains why she reads the city this way. She owns the account of her earlier danger and household collapse. The film does not stage violence for spectacle. Her testimony changes the audience’s map of Puhar: decorated public spaces now contain routes of exposure, refuge and unequal protection.

Before Udayakumaran enters, the public gaze is already upon Manimekalai. People recognise Madhavi’s daughter. Her trained posture, balance and attention—skills inherited from an artistic household—make her visible even when she does not perform. The camera does not convert that visibility into entitlement. Beauty is shown as exposure.

Udayakumaran sees her. His attraction is sincere, immediate and intensified by rank. He is not introduced as a monster. He is accustomed to access, accustomed to the city arranging itself around royal movement, and insufficiently trained to understand that desire does not create reciprocity.

The garden and crystal-pavilion movement becomes an escalating test of whether refusal will be heard. Sudhamathi reads the prince’s approach and urges concealment or departure. Manimekalai resists being moved as though she has already surrendered control. Their disagreement is brief but real. They act together because danger, not obedience, requires it.

When Udayakumaran reaches Manimekalai, she refuses him clearly. The scene does not depend on coded reluctance or romantic misunderstanding. She does not insult his humanity; she denies his claim. He hears the words but interprets refusal as an obstacle within a story whose ending he still imagines himself entitled to reach.

A supernatural warning reaches him at a palace threshold. The warning is not a mechanism that removes his responsibility. It creates a final opportunity for restraint. It connects self-command with the discipline required of a future ruler. Udayakumaran is shaken, but not transformed. He allows desire, karmic suggestion and royal possibility to remain stronger than the warning.

Night falls over Puhar. The goddess Manimekala intervenes, not as decorative spectacle but as an authority choosing to remove Manimekalai from immediate danger. The transition is physically disorienting. Manimekalai does not command it. Escape is not triumph; it is loss of place, knowledge and control.

In Puhar, Sudhamathi finds the space beside her empty. The film remains with the human cost before cutting to sacred geography. She searches, reconstructs the last moments and returns to Madhavi. The former performer’s disciplined household routines break under maternal panic. Sudhamathi must give an account before older women who are tempted to complete it for her. She insists on what she saw, what she inferred and what she does not know.

**Sequence exit:** Udayakumaran’s pursuit remains active, while Manimekalai has been removed from the city into a place where no familiar social identity can protect her.

# SEQ-03 — The Island of Memory

Manimekalai wakes alone on Manipallavam.

The island is not staged as an ornamental fantasy realm. Wind, tide, birds, sun and the absence of a city establish its scale. She tests her body, checks for injury, calls for Sudhamathi and receives no answer. Sacred isolation first produces fear.

She moves across the shore using ordinary observation before revelation begins. The film allows silence and bodily uncertainty to remain. Her training helps her balance and orient herself, but it cannot explain where she is or why she has been taken.

At the Gomukhi pond and the sacred seat, memory opens. Previous-life connection is revealed in layers rather than delivered as complete cosmology. Manimekalai experiences continuity involving Udayakumaran, but the film separates memory from permission. The revelation may explain emotional force; it does not create a present claim upon her. She recognises that a past relation can be real without governing current consent.

Deepathilahai guides her toward the Amudhasurabhi. The vessel’s appearance is quiet. It is not framed as treasure, weapon or reward for purity. Manimekalai initially experiences wonder, then the weight of what the object means. An inexhaustible capacity to feed is not freedom from responsibility; it is responsibility toward people she has not met.

She accepts the vessel without understanding how to use it socially. The film refuses instant sainthood. She still lacks a system, a community of labour and a tested philosophy. She has received obligation, not completion.

The movement back toward Puhar is joined to Sudhamathi’s testimony. Rather than allowing the sacred island to replace human knowledge, the film crosscuts Manimekalai’s experience with Sudhamathi reconstructing the disappearance before Madhavi. Sudhamathi distinguishes observation from inference. She saw the goddess’s intervention only within the limits available to her; she cannot describe Manipallavam. This prepares the film’s later philosophical concern with testimony and proof.

Manimekalai returns carrying the vessel into a city that still knows her primarily as Madhavi’s daughter and the object of a prince’s desire. She now knows that her life touches histories larger than Puhar, but she does not yet know how sacred memory should become public action.

**Act I exit:** Manimekalai has moved from refusal to responsibility. The question is no longer only whether she can escape an inherited destiny, but what she will do with a power that cannot ethically remain private.
