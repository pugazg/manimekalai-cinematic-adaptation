## Perspective frame

The Venpa perspective gives Kayasandihai's hunger and relief independent moral importance. Her suffering must not exist only to provide Manimekalai with a disguise or to trigger Kanchanan's mistaken inference.

The later disguise and killing chain should therefore preserve four distinctions:

1. Kayasandihai is a person with a life before, during and after hunger.
2. Manimekalai may bear Kayasandihai's form without acquiring her identity, relationships or obligations.
3. Kanchanan's long search may explain his expectation without making recognition a debt owed by the woman he sees.
4. Prior causes, partial vision or mistaken identity do not move the sword; Kanchanan does.

Exact verse headings and line references for the disguise-and-killing movement remain subject to verification against a reliable page image or printed edition before any direct quotation or dialogue adaptation.

## Executive finding

The current screenplay's fatal-inference architecture is already unusually strong. It does not treat Udayakumaran's death as a sudden accident produced by one misunderstood glance.

It gives Kanchanan:

- years of searching and genuine loyalty;
- an expectation that search must be rewarded by recognition;
- a direct statement from Manimekalai that she is not the person he names;
- incomplete visual and audible information;
- a conclusion that answers his fear but has no confirming witness;
- several available alternatives before violence;
- deliberate preparation with the sword;
- correction without absolution after the killing.

It also explicitly protects Manimekalai from inherited responsibility. She refuses Udayakumaran, attempts to move danger away from sleeping recipients, cannot safely disclose the disguise, hears the weapon too late and states that she did not want the prince dead.

A broad rewrite is therefore **not recommended**.

The main gap is earlier: Scene `#33#` asks Kayasandihai what she needs after hunger releases, but the screenplay supplies no answer. She then disappears from the active narrative and returns mainly as Kanchanan's memory and Manimekalai's borrowed form. One short response can preserve Kayasandihai as a person moving into a life beyond hunger.

## Scene findings

### Scene #33 — FU-023 / SC-019

Kayasandihai's hunger is treated without comedy or miraculous spectacle. Her breathing, posture and attention return gradually. Manimekalai asks what she needs now, correctly recognising that food relief is not the end of care.

**Finding:** Strong recovery scene with an intentionally open final question.  
**Gap:** Kayasandihai is not allowed to answer.  
**Recommended approach:** Add one concrete response concerned with water, rest and information about the life or roads beyond the feeding space. Do not make reunion with Kanchanan her automatic next destination.

### Scene #37 — BR-009 / SC-131 / ABSORBS SC-129, SC-130

The montage establishes that Kanchanan's loyalty is real while also naming the expectation that years of searching must produce recognition. This distinction is essential.

**Finding:** Strong.  
**Risk:** Because the montage belongs entirely to Kanchanan's memory, Kayasandihai can become an object around which his loyalty is measured.  
**Recommended approach:** Do not add material until the exact Venpa account of Kayasandihai's movement away from him is source-verified.

### Scene #38 — FU-028 / SC-132 / ABSORBS SC-007, SC-060

The line `The disguise changes her face, voice and danger. It does not give her Kayasandihai's history.` is the governing safeguard for the entire chain.

Manimekalai directly says that Kanchanan has mistaken her and that she is not the person he names. His reinterpretation of those statements as anger or secrecy is shown as his choice.

**Finding:** Strong.  
**Recommended change:** None.  
**Safeguard:** Do not give Manimekalai dialogue that sounds like she accepts Kayasandihai's marital identity in order to preserve the disguise.

### Scene #39 — FU-029 / SC-133 / ABSORBS SC-093

Kanchanan sees Udayakumaran following a woman with Kayasandihai's face. He misses the beginning of the exchange and hears only selected fragments. The screenplay then distinguishes what he sees from the meaning he supplies.

**Finding:** Strong fatal-inference construction.  
**Recommended change:** None.  
**Safeguard:** Preserve Manimekalai's complete refusal and the absence of any confirming witness.

### Scene #40 — FU-030 / SC-134

The scene provides exits, witnesses, daylight confrontation and a direct question as available alternatives. Kanchanan rejects all of them and prepares the blade.

**Finding:** Essential accountability scene.  
**Recommended change:** None.  
**Safeguard:** Do not shorten this scene into a sudden burst of jealousy. Preparation is what prevents grief from becoming an excuse.

### Scene #41 — FU-031 / SC-135

Udayakumaran again enters after refusal and removes witnesses while believing he has removed rank. Kanchanan strikes from concealment. Manimekalai hears the blade too late.

**Finding:** Clear intersecting responsibility.  
**Recommended change:** None.  
**Safeguard:** Do not stage a duel or heroic defence that shifts the killing from inference and choice into combat.

### Scene #42 — FU-032 / SC-136

The Kanthil Pavai separates an older causal chain from the immediate hand that used the sword. It also corrects Kanchanan's belief without granting absolution.

**Finding:** Strong doctrinal and moral distinction.  
**Recommended change:** None until the corresponding source passage and terminology are specialist-reviewed.

### Scene #43 — FU-033 / SC-069 / ABSORBS SC-096

Sudhamathi states the sequence's moral conclusion: Kanchanan's grief explains the road but did not move his hand. Manimekalai states that she did not want Udayakumaran dead, while the screenplay refuses to turn his pursuit into her act.

**Finding:** Strong.  
**Recommended change:** None.
