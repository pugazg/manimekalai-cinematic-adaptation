## Source-perspective findings

The Venpa does not present Udayakumaran's attraction as a single misunderstood encounter. It gives the movement duration, warning, fixation and consequence:

1. Ettikumaran tells the prince about Manimekalai and her renunciant life.
2. Udayakumaran immediately declares that he will bring her in his chariot and considers time spent without approaching her wasted.
3. His perception becomes saturated: he sees Manimekalai along the road and moves toward her despite prior knowledge of her chosen life.
4. Sudhamathi identifies desire as a destructive fire and challenges his authority to change Manimekalai's mind.
5. The prince treats obtaining Manimekalai as an achievable personal objective rather than a decision belonging to her.
6. Later, he continues to approach the place where she serves, even after warning and concealment.
7. Kanchanan's mistaken inference and violence end the prince's life.
8. Manimekalai grieves after his death.

Relevant OCR headings and movements include:

- `உதயகுமரன் மணிமேகலை பற்றிக் கேள்விப்படுகிறான்`
- `மணிமேகலையை என்தேரில் ஏற்றி வந்துவிடுவேன்`
- `உதயனின் கண்ணுக்கு வழியெல்லாம் மணிமேகலை`
- `காமம் ஒரு தீ !`
- `மணிமேகலை மனத்தை மாற்ற நீ யார்?`
- `மணிமேகலையை நான் அடைவது சின்னது`
- `உதயனின் ஐயப்பாடு`
- `உதயனைக் காஞ்சனன் வெட்டி வீழ்த்தினான்`
- `இறந்த உதயனுக்கு அழுதாள் மணிமேகலை`

The Venpa's grief language is emotionally valuable, but close adaptation must not make Manimekalai's sorrow into an admission that her thought, refusal or disguise caused the killing. ## Executive finding

The current screenplay already avoids the most serious adaptation error: it does not romanticise persistence after refusal.

The accountability chain is explicit:

- Scene `#9#` shows that the prince's public authority makes Manimekalai visible before she chooses to be seen.
- Scene `#10#` gives Manimekalai a complete refusal: her life is not a path another person may choose for her.
- Scene `#11#` warns that undisciplined desire becomes danger; the prince hears but does not change.
- Scene `#35#` gives Chitrapathi a materially grounded fear while preventing that fear from becoming permission.
- Scene `#36#` distinguishes previous-life cause from present permission.
- Scene `#39#` records another refusal and an order not to enter the sleeping court.
- Scene `#41#` shows the prince returning alone after warning and believing the removal of attendants removes rank.
- His final words still expect time to change Manimekalai rather than himself.
- Scene `#43#` separates Manimekalai's grief from responsibility for his pursuit or death.
- Scenes `#44#`–`#45#` refuse to merge accounts or call his death proof of virtuous love.
- Scenes `#50#`–`#53#` prevent a mother's grief from becoming authority to punish another body.

A broad rewrite is therefore **not recommended**.

The main remaining opportunities are:

1. make the accumulated refusals and warnings visible as evidence at the hearing, rather than relying on the audience to reconstruct them;
2. give Manimekalai one sentence of grief that cannot be mistaken for retrospective consent;
3. let Vasantavai preserve Rajamadevi's right to mourn her son while refusing to make pursuit his memorial virtue.

## Scene findings

### Scenes #9–#11 — FU-008 through FU-010

Udayakumaran's first appearance is already framed through unequal visibility. Manimekalai's refusal is complete, and the warning names the foreseeable consequence of undisciplined desire.

**Finding:** Strong foundation.  
**Recommended structural change:** None.  
**Safeguard:** Do not soften the refusal into uncertainty merely to create romantic suspense.

### Scene #35 — FU-025 / SC-107

Chitrapathi seeks protection for an economically vulnerable artistic household. Her fear is real, but the scene exposes the moment when Udayakumaran converts her hesitation into permission. She warns him not to make her fear his right.

**Finding:** Strong and morally complex.  
**Recommended change:** None.  
**Safeguard:** Do not make Chitrapathi the sole cause of his later conduct; the warning reaches him and what follows remains his choice.

### Scene #36 — FU-026 / SC-092

Manimekalai says that cause is not permission and that previous-life familiarity cannot decide what a living person owes. Udayakumaran responds by promising to wait until she understands him, still treating her eventual understanding as the proper end of his story.

**Finding:** Essential.  
**Recommended change:** None.

### Scenes #39–#40 — FU-029 / SC-133 and FU-030 / SC-134

Manimekalai orders Udayakumaran not to enter the sleeping court again. Kanchanan hears fragments and prepares violence, but the prince's own repeated entry remains independently established.

**Finding:** Strong parallel accountability.  
**Recommended change:** None.  
**Safeguard:** Kanchanan's inference must not erase Udayakumaran's prior choices, and Udayakumaran's pursuit must not excuse Kanchanan's sword.

### Scene #41 — FU-031 / SC-135

The prince enters alone and claims to have come without rank. Manimekalai correctly states that rank is not removed by dismissing those who announce it. His final expectation is that time should change her.

**Finding:** Strong tragic culmination.  
**Recommended change:** No deathbed redemption or mutual-love declaration.  
**Safeguard:** Death must not grant the consent he did not receive while alive.

### Scene #43 — FU-033 / SC-069 / ABSORBS SC-096

Manimekalai states that she did not want him dead. Sudhamathi separates his pursuit from her act and Kanchanan's grief from the hand that struck.

**Finding:** Strong protection against victim-blaming.  
**Opportunity:** Add one sentence permitting grief while refusing the romantic renaming of pursuit.

### Scene #44 — FU-034 / SC-050

The hearing keeps Kanchanan's action, Udayakumaran's entry and Manimekalai's refusal in separate accounts. A relief worker testifies that the prince had been asked not to return.

**Finding:** Strong evidentiary architecture.  
**Opportunity:** Place the earlier warnings and refusals in a visible chronological record.

### Scene #45 — FU-035 / SC-112 / ABSORBS SC-052, SC-094, SC-111

Rajamadevi asks that her son be said to have died for love. Vasantavai refuses to make persistence virtuous because the body is warm and distinguishes gentleness from respect for another person's answer.

**Finding:** Strong grief correction.  
**Opportunity:** Give the Queen permission to mourn the son without making his pursuit the form in which he is remembered.

### Scenes #50–#53 — FU-040 through FU-043

Manimekalai and Rajamadevi separate grief from what grief commands. The Queen eventually identifies her own orders and power publicly.

**Finding:** Strong.  
**Recommended change:** None beyond any approved Scene `#45#` memorial distinction.

### VENPA-USE-029

**Element:** Add a deathbed declaration of mutual love, a Lakshmi–Ragulan reunion, a kiss, or a final admission that Manimekalai secretly wanted Udayakumaran.  
**Use type:** `DIRECT`, `ADAPTED` or `THEMATIC`  
**Status:** `REJECTED`

**Reason:** Death cannot supply consent that was refused while he lived. Such a scene would transform repeated pursuit into romantic destiny, reward persistence and make Manimekalai's autonomy temporary.

### VENPA-USE-030

**Element:** Closely adapt the grief verse so that Manimekalai states or implies that her thought, fear, disguise or refusal caused Udayakumaran's death.  
**Use type:** `DIRECT`  
**Status:** `REJECTED`

**Reason:** Manimekalai may grieve and may acknowledge that their lives intersected. Kanchanan chose the sword; Udayakumaran chose repeated entry after warning. Her refusal and protective disguise are not acts of killing.

### VENPA-USE-031

**Element:** Present past-life fate, karma or supernatural necessity as directly moving Udayakumaran toward the court or Kanchanan's hand toward the sword.  
**Use type:** `DIRECT` or `THEMATIC`  
**Status:** `REJECTED`

**Reason:** The screenplay's governing distinction is that previous causes create conditions but do not remove present choice. Fate may explain convergence; it must not erase accountability.
