## Source-perspective findings

The Venpa prison movement does not isolate Manimekalai inside a private conflict with authority. It repeatedly keeps the public and lower-level institutional workers visible:

1. People protest the order to imprison her.
2. Manimekalai addresses the public about delegated obedience, harmful conduct and moral responsibility.
3. A public stopping place is converted into a guarded confinement space.
4. People remain angry after the confinement begins.
5. Ordinary residents bring food.
6. Prison workers report what they were ordered to provide and sometimes act beyond the minimum order.
7. The prisoner's condition becomes information that travels back toward royal power.
8. Elsewhere, the Venpa compares feeding those without resources to a learned person giving education to those without learning.

- `அரசுக்கு எதிர்ப்பு`

## Executive finding

The current screenplay already develops a sophisticated institutional arc.

Scene `#34#` does more than replace punishment with charity. It:

- opens rooms rather than admitting another prisoner;
- removes restraints;
- creates sleeping, cooking and storage infrastructure;
- gives the first ladle to a former prisoner with lived knowledge of the building;
- asks what the institution will teach after it stops teaching fear;
- states that people, not the vessel alone, reorganise power.

The later sequences then test whether that transformation can survive crisis:

- in Scene `#43#`, royal guards order recipients aside and invoke the transformed prison again as a destination;
- in Scene `#44#`, a relief worker becomes a court witness and the king insists that separate accounts remain separate;
- in Scene `#46#`, a public record says Manimekalai is released while a private bolt confines her;
- in Scenes `#47#–#49#`, harm is distributed through kitchens, carriers, intermediaries, guards, ration records and withheld labour;
- in Scenes `#50#–#53#`, small witnesses connect evidence and royal accountability becomes public record.

A broad rewrite is therefore **not recommended**.

The main gap is continuity. Scene `#34#` transforms the building, but no visible public record or learning practice preserves what the transformation means. When state power reasserts itself in Scene `#43#`, the former prisoner and the reformed institution disappear from the causal chain. At the end of Scene `#53#`, the Queen records past abuses but does not yet state the rule that would prevent a future official from calling private confinement “release” again.

## Scene findings

### Scene #34 — FU-024 / SC-020 / ABSORBS SC-037, SC-051

The former prison becomes shelter, kitchen and shared infrastructure. The former prisoner receives the first ladle because lived knowledge outranks official distance.

**Finding:** Strong transformation scene.  
**Gap:** `What will this place teach now?` is answered ethically, but education and institutional memory remain abstract.  
**Recommended approach:** Add one compact public records-and-learning beat. Do not turn the scene into a modern classroom or a long policy explanation.

### Scene #43 — FU-033 / SC-069 / ABSORBS SC-096

The people Manimekalai fed are ordered aside, and the prison she transformed is invoked again as a destination.

**Finding:** Strong demonstration that reform is fragile under grief and state urgency.  
**Gap:** The transformed institution has no visible custodian who preserves a record of the attempted reversal.  
**Recommended approach:** Let the former prisoner or another relief custodian record the guards, order and stated destination. The arrest may proceed, but not anonymously.

### Scene #44 — FU-034 / SC-050

A relief worker appears as a witness, Kanchanan gives an unornamented account and the king refuses to fold separate actions into one story.

**Finding:** Strong hearing architecture.  
**Recommended change:** None.  
**Safeguard:** Do not make the hearing depend only on royal wisdom; retain workers and scribes as independent evidence carriers.

### Scene #46 — FU-036 / SC-113 / ABSORBS SC-062

The public record describes release while the private room is bolted. The scene precisely shows how confinement can be produced by conflicting administrative descriptions of one door.

**Finding:** Essential institutional critique.  
**Recommended structural change:** None.  
**Later consequence required:** Scene `#53#` should convert this discovery into a durable rule, not only a confession.

### Scenes #47–#49 — FU-037 through FU-039

The poisoning attempt, purchased accusation and organised deprivation show that no single guard needs to announce a prison sentence. Harm is made through routine obedience and fragmented records. The counterforce is also institutional: servants preserve evidence, a water carrier notices measures and a scribe finds contradictions.

**Finding:** Strong and Venpa-compatible in principle.  
**Recommended change:** None.  
**Key strength:** Workers are not treated as morally empty extensions of power. They can carry harm, refuse it, preserve evidence or become witnesses.

### Scenes #50–#53 — FU-040 through FU-043

The Queen learns to distinguish grief from what grief commands. The gate opens because evidence and denial are both recordable. At the final threshold, she publicly identifies her own power as the condition that made obedience safer than refusal.

**Finding:** Strong accountability arc.  
**Opportunity:** Add one prospective rule addressing contradictory custody records, hidden bolts and withheld provisions.

### VENPA-USE-025

**Element:** Reproduce the Venpa's public anger through a crowd physically breaking guards' weapons and forcibly preventing Manimekalai's detention.  
**Use type:** `DIRECT`  
**Status:** `REJECTED`

**Reason:** The current screenplay's stronger through-line is that public records, workers, witnesses and separate accounts constrain state power. A violent rescue would prevent the later hearing and palace-accountability arc, risk turning the crowd into spectacle and weaken Manimekalai's insistence that responsibility be heard rather than collapsed into grief.
