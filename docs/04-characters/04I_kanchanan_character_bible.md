# 04I — Kanchanan Character Bible

## Performance and Screenwriting Edition 0.1

**Project:** *Manimekalai* cinematic adaptation  
**Character:** Kanchanan / Kānchanan  
**Status:** Working character edition. Exact Tamil name form, title, Vidyādhara or Vinjayan terminology, age, cosmological identity, marriage vocabulary, weapon, wound choreography, travel mechanics and post-killing fate remain subject to original-text and specialist review.

---

## 1. Dramatic identity

### One-line character statement

Kanchanan is a sky-world traveller who supports his wife through a twelve-year curse and returns faithfully to her, but destroys an innocent life when love hardened by separation becomes possession and observation becomes certainty without inquiry.

### Core dramatic movement

**Loving travelling husband → caregiver during curse → recurring visitor through separation → hopeful return → mistaken recognition → wounded pride → jealous interpretation → concealed armed watcher → lethal certainty → divine correction → grief without repair → departure under consequence**

### Central contradiction

Kanchanan is capable of tenderness, loyalty and sustained care. Yet when the woman before him does not respond as expected, he trusts the rights he believes marriage gives him more than the evidence of her changed behaviour.

He must distinguish:

- recognition from resemblance;
- marriage from ownership;
- long care from permanent entitlement;
- silence from betrayal;
- observation from proof;
- suspicion from knowledge;
- protection from control;
- fate from personal responsibility;
- and remorse from repair.

### False solution

**[INTERPRETATION]** Kanchanan believes immediate violence can restore an intelligible world: remove the rival, recover the wife and erase the humiliation of being treated as a stranger.

The killing produces the opposite. The supposed rival dies, the woman is revealed not to be his wife and he learns that Kayasandihai has already died beyond his reach.

### Deeper need

To accept that love cannot make another person's identity self-evident and that uncertainty must be endured long enough for truth to be spoken.

### End-state

The poem gives him knowledge, sorrow and departure—not completed restitution. The adaptation must preserve that incompleteness. His recognition begins after irreversible harm; it does not transform the killing into a necessary lesson or make grief a sufficient payment.

## 2. Evidence discipline

Every character claim must use one of the following labels:

- **[TEXT]** — directly stated or strongly implied in *Manimekalai*.
- **[CROSS]** — supported by another early Tamil source after verification.
- **[HISTORY]** — supported by responsible historical research.
- **[INTERPRETATION]** — controlled psychological or performance reconstruction.
- **[CAUTION]** — a modern assumption that must not be presented as ancient fact.

### Character-bible limits

This document does not establish:

- one final spelling or etymology for Kanchanan's name;
- that “Vidyādhara,” “Vinjayan,” “apsaras,” “sky dweller” and every translated supernatural category are interchangeable;
- a modern ethnicity, race or biological species for his community;
- his exact numerical age;
- the exact legal or social structure of his marriage;
- that twelve years of care entitle him to obedience or sexual access;
- the exact form, weight or historical type of his sword;
- the final choreography or anatomy of Udayakumaran's wound;
- that fate removes intention, choice or responsibility;
- or an unrecorded redemption after the text says he leaves in sorrow.

## 3. Life stage and casting

### Textual position

**[TEXT]** Kanchanan and Kayasandihai travel together from Kanchanapuram, a northern place associated with the Vidyādharas, to the Potiya mountain region. He remains involved after her curse, brings food, proposes a route toward help and returns during Indra's festival while counting the years until the curse ends.

### Age rule

**[CAUTION]** No exact age is approved. Cast an adult performer capable of embodying:

- an established marriage or long partnership;
- repeated travel over many years;
- practical caregiving;
- supernatural mobility without superhero swagger;
- deep hope at reunion;
- rapid but intelligible cognitive error;
- trained weapon familiarity;
- and the collapse that follows irreversible knowledge.

### Required actor capacities

- tenderness that does not predetermine innocence;
- active listening failures visible in behaviour;
- jealousy performed through perception and interpretation rather than theatrical madness;
- physical competence without heroic glamour;
- ability to carry silence before violence;
- shock when identity and bereavement are revealed together;
- and remorse that remains morally insufficient.

### Casting separation from Udayakumaran

The two men must not become visual doubles whose conflict reads as a simple romantic contest.

Kanchanan's body and movement should reflect:

- travel;
- different cosmological upbringing;
- familiarity with aerial movement;
- long separation;
- and direct weapon competence.

Udayakumaran's body reflects royal access, chariot culture and pursuit within Puhar. Their failures overlap in entitlement but are not identical.

## 4. Established narrative history

### Journey with Kayasandihai

**[TEXT]** Kayasandihai says she came with her husband Kanchanan from Kanchanapuram in the Vidyādhara world to see the Potiya mountains in the south.

They are together when the sage Viruchigan's fruit is destroyed and the curse is imposed.

### Care during the curse

**[TEXT]** Kanchanan is frightened by her condition. He asks her to travel with him through the sky, learns that she has forgotten the formula or power required for flight and brings fruits, roots and other foods that fail to satisfy her hunger.

He does not immediately abandon her.

### Route toward help

**[TEXT]** He tells Kayasandihai that wealthy people near the Tamil country feed and assist suffering people and that she can reach the region by walking over land.

This act combines:

- practical knowledge;
- inability to cure her himself;
- trust in human generosity;
- and the beginning of long physical separation.

### Annual return

**[TEXT]** Kayasandihai says Kanchanan visits on the day of Indra's festival and leaves counting the time until her curse will end.

The exact number and continuity of visits require Tamil review, but the text establishes repeated return and expectation.

### Kayasandihai's cure and departure

**[TEXT]** Manimekalai feeds Kayasandihai from the Amudhasurabhi and the insatiable hunger ends. Kayasandihai believes the twelve-year term is complete and leaves for the sky world after directing Manimekalai toward the Ulaga Aravi.

### Death beyond his knowledge

**[TEXT]** The pillar figure later tells Kanchanan that Kayasandihai attempted to return through the Vindhya region and was swallowed by its guardian goddess.

Kanchanan therefore arrives expecting reunion without knowing that his wife is dead.

### Arrival at the temple

**[TEXT]** Kanchanan sees Manimekalai in Kayasandihai's form, approaches lovingly, recalls their years together and praises her.

The person before him does not respond as he expects and turns toward Udayakumaran.

### Jealous inference

**[TEXT]** Watching Manimekalai speak to the prince while wearing Kayasandihai's appearance, Kanchanan interprets her lack of response, speech, smiles, gaze and physical beauty as evidence that the prince is her lover.

The poem gives his inference. It does not make the inference true.

### Concealment

**[TEXT]** His anger burns, he enters the temple with a sword and hides like a snake in a hole.

This interval matters. The killing is not represented only as an instantaneous reflex. He conceals himself and waits.

### Killing

**[TEXT]** Udayakumaran returns in the middle of the night. Kanchanan believes the prince has come for his wife, rises behind him and strikes with the sword.

The supplied translation describes a severe cutting injury but its exact anatomy and action require original-Tamil review before choreography is fixed.

### Revelation

**[TEXT]** When Kanchanan then moves toward the woman he believes to be Kayasandihai, the Kanthil Pavai stops him and says:

- she is Manimekalai in disguise;
- Kayasandihai's hunger had ended;
- Kayasandihai died while attempting to return through the Vindhya region;
- Udayakumaran's fate brought him to death;
- Kanchanan nevertheless committed a cruel act;
- and the consequences of that act will come to him.

### Departure

**[TEXT]** Kanchanan rises sorrowing for what he has done and returns to his sky world.

The text does not supply a completed legal process, apology to the royal household, reconciliation with Manimekalai or visible later reform.

## 5. Relationship with Kayasandihai

### Real care

Kanchanan's care must remain visible:

- fear when the curse begins;
- effort to take her home;
- unsuccessful attempts to feed her;
- knowledge of routes and charitable communities;
- repeated visits;
- and anticipation of release.

Removing this care would make the later violence easy but dramatically shallow.

### Care is not ownership

The same history cannot be used to argue that Kayasandihai owes him:

- immediate recognition;
- unchanged affection;
- explanation before safety;
- sexual availability;
- or freedom from relationships he has not verified.

The central error is not that he loved her too much. It is that he treats the history of love as proof that the body before him must answer to his expectations.

### Kayasandihai retains her own story

She is not only “Kanchanan's wife.” Her dossier relationship must preserve:

- her action that triggers the curse;
- twelve years of hunger;
- dependence and mobility loss;
- knowledge of charitable institutions;
- encounter with Manimekalai;
- cure;
- guidance toward the Ulaga Aravi;
- independent departure;
- and death beyond Kanchanan's presence.

### Reunion that never occurs

Kanchanan's tragedy includes arriving after the relationship has already ended through death while believing reunion is moments away.

The adaptation may build anticipation through:

- counted festival cycles;
- remembered phrases;
- an object or travel habit after review;
- and uncertainty over whether Kayasandihai can still return.

It must not invent secret infidelity or a marital breakdown to justify suspicion.

## 6. Perception, uncertainty and mistaken identity

### What he actually observes

Kanchanan observes:

- a body and face matching Kayasandihai;
- the apparent wife not responding warmly to him;
- her turning toward a royal man;
- intimate or morally serious conversation;
- smiles, gaze and expressive speech as interpreted by him;
- and the prince returning at night.

### What he does not know

He does not know:

- that Manimekalai can transform her appearance;
- why she chose Kayasandihai's form;
- that Kayasandihai has already left Puhar;
- that she died near the Vindhya mountain;
- Manimekalai's history with Udayakumaran;
- the goddess's warning to the prince;
- or the prince's intended action that night.

### The epistemic failure

Kanchanan does not lack every piece of evidence. He lacks enough evidence for certainty.

The character's failure is the conversion:

**resemblance → recognition → entitlement → suspicion → accusation without speech → lethal judgement**

### No audience superiority shortcut

The audience may know the disguise before Kanchanan arrives. The scene must still allow viewers to understand how the visible evidence misleads him without inviting them to approve the conclusion.

### Questions he does not ask

The actor should carry the absent questions:

- Why does she respond like a stranger?
- Is she ill, frightened, enchanted or transformed?
- Why is she feeding people here?
- What happened during the missing years?
- Who is the prince?
- Who else can identify her?
- Why does her speech not sound like memory?

His refusal to pause is more important than portraying jealousy as uncontrollable magic.

## 7. Jealousy, masculinity and possession

### Jealousy is not proof of love

The film must not use jealousy as cinematic shorthand for devotion.

Kanchanan's jealousy contains:

- fear of loss;
- humiliation;
- accumulated separation;
- desire for restored certainty;
- possessiveness;
- and readiness to make another man's death the solution.

### No “honour killing” romance

Do not frame the act through:

- wounded masculine honour as a noble code;
- triumphant protection of marriage;
- slow-motion righteous violence;
- the prince as a trophy rival;
- or Kayasandihai's apparent beauty as provocation.

### His weapon matters

He arrives armed and knows how to use the weapon. The sword is not created by jealousy at the moment of crisis.

Production must ask:

- why he travels armed;
- whether the weapon is ordinary to his status;
- how concealment changes intent;
- and what opportunities existed to leave, confront, warn or seek witnesses.

### Caregiver entitlement

A particularly important performance danger is the idea:

> “After everything I did for you, you owe me.”

The text does not need this exact line. The body may express it when expected gratitude or intimacy is absent.

## 8. Relationship with Manimekalai

### She is wearing his wife's form

Manimekalai's disguise creates a serious ethical and dramatic problem. She does not intend to deceive Kanchanan specifically, but her protective act places another woman's face inside a new social context.

The film must preserve:

- Manimekalai's need to escape Udayakumaran;
- her incomplete knowledge of Kanchanan's arrival;
- the danger of assuming another woman's relationships;
- and the fact that none of this makes her responsible for Kanchanan's killing.

### No forced intimacy

Kanchanan's belief that she is his wife does not give the production permission to stage unwanted embrace, touch or sexual proximity without clear consent and intimacy coordination.

If he approaches physically, the scene should prioritise:

- Manimekalai's uncertainty;
- her attempt to create distance;
- the risk of revealing the disguise;
- and her assessment of public danger.

### After revelation

Kanchanan learns that the person he has placed in mortal danger is not Kayasandihai.

The text does not provide a full exchange between him and Manimekalai. Do not invent easy forgiveness or make her responsible for consoling him over his wife's death and his own act.

---

## 9. Relationship with Udayakumaran

### Two distinct failures

Udayakumaran fails to respect Manimekalai's refusal and returns in pursuit.

Kanchanan fails to investigate and chooses lethal violence.

The prince's pursuit does not justify the killing. Kanchanan's violence does not retroactively make the prince's pursuit innocent.

### No romantic triangle

This is not fundamentally a contest between two men for one woman.

The woman Kanchanan thinks is present is dead. The woman actually present has refused the prince and has not chosen Kanchanan.

The triangle exists only inside misrecognition and male claims.

### The ambush

The supplied translation says Kanchanan rises behind the prince and strikes. The violence should communicate:

- asymmetric knowledge;
- concealment;
- lack of warning;
- speed;
- and irreversible consequence.

Do not turn the scene into an extended equal duel invented to make the killing heroic or commercially exciting.

### Fate and responsibility

Later speakers describe both men as brought together by fate or karma. The adaptation's binding rule remains:

> Causal fate can explain the convergence without removing the moral ownership of present action.

---

## 10. Relationship with the Kanthil Pavai

### The interruption

The pillar figure stops Kanchanan before he approaches Manimekalai after the killing.

This is a second threshold:

- before the voice, he believes he has removed a rival;
- after the voice, wife, victim and supposed evidence all change meaning.

### Revelation is not acquittal

The divine figure says Udayakumaran's fate brought his death but also explicitly identifies Kanchanan's act as cruel and predicts consequence.

Do not retain only the fate statement while dropping responsibility.

### Performance of receiving truth

The revelation arrives in stages:

1. the woman is not Kayasandihai;
2. the real Kayasandihai's hunger ended;
3. she attempted to return;
4. she was killed by the Vindhya guardian;
5. Udayakumaran had his own causal past;
6. Kanchanan has committed a cruel act;
7. consequence continues.

The actor should not jump directly to generic remorse. Each fact destroys a different assumption.

### No magical moral completion

Hearing divine truth does not automatically teach him:

- how to grieve without possession;
- how to answer to the dead prince's family;
- how to face Manimekalai;
- or how to live with consequence.

## 11. Voice system

### With Kayasandihai during the curse

- practical;
- affectionate;
- worried;
- offering routes and food rather than abstract reassurance;
- and capable of admitting that he cannot cure her.

### At annual visits

- compressed by limited time;
- attentive to visible health;
- counting years;
- carrying hope and fatigue;
- and possibly avoiding questions whose answers may threaten the relationship.

### First approach to disguised Manimekalai

- relief before suspicion;
- remembered intimacy;
- praise shaped by expectation;
- and confusion when response does not match memory.

### Jealous interpretation

His speech may reduce as observation hardens. Silence should not be treated as calm. It becomes a closed system in which he no longer risks correction.

### Before violence

No grand declaration is required. The danger lies in judgement completed internally.

### After revelation

Words may fragment around:

- denial;
- his wife's name;
- the route she took;
- what he believed he saw;
- and the impossibility of undoing the strike.

Do not give him a polished speech that converts the scene into his emotional redemption.

## 12. Movement and combat system

### Sky travel

No final VFX grammar is approved.

Avoid:

- superhero landing poses;
- effortless high-speed combat flight;
- winged-race stereotypes;
- or visual coding borrowed wholesale from later pan-Indian fantasy media.

Movement must be developed from:

- textual verbs;
- the existing divine-transport grammar;
- weather and distance;
- fatigue;
- costume behaviour;
- and cosmological distinction without biological racialisation.

### Caregiving movement

Earlier scenes may show:

- carrying food that fails to satisfy;
- supporting without infantilising Kayasandihai;
- assessing whether she can travel;
- and leaving reluctantly after festival visits.

### Arrival in Puhar

His body should initially move toward reunion, not battle.

The transition to danger occurs through:

- failed recognition;
- observation from a distance;
- possession of the sword;
- concealment;
- and waiting.

### Killing choreography

Binding safeguards:

- verify the original Tamil wound before final blocking;
- no extended duel unless explicitly logged as adaptation;
- no glamorous sword flourish;
- no erotic intercutting with the disguised woman;
- no repeated impact shots;
- and preserve Manimekalai's position, perception and danger.

### After truth

His body should lose the certainty that organised the attack. Do not replace it immediately with devotional serenity.

## 13. Costume, objects and cosmology

### Costume rule

Kanchanan and Kayasandihai require a jointly researched visual system, but not identical costumes.

Do not automatically use:

- North Indian celestial-court costume;
- medieval royal jewellery;
- wings;
- pale or metallic skin;
- tribal-fantasy coding;
- or costume difference as evidence of nonhuman moral otherness.

### Costume continuity

Track:

- travel wear;
- annual festival visits;
- weather and repair;
- sword suspension;
- garlands or ornament named by the text;
- and whether sky-world materials are historically reconstructable or cinematic interpretation.

### The sword

The weapon requires:

- philological review of the Tamil term;
- historical-arms consultation without assuming human archaeological equivalence;
- safe stunt design;
- clear continuity during concealment and strike;
- and no merchandising or publicity image that glorifies the killing.

### Kanchanapuram and sky world

No exact map or architecture is approved. The setting must remain cosmological and culturally specific without becoming generic golden-cloud fantasy.

## 14. Violence and safeguarding

### Lethal jealousy

The sequence involves intimate-partner possessiveness, mistaken identity and lethal violence against a perceived rival.

Production review should include:

- gender-violence specialists;
- coercive-control consultation;
- stunt and weapons safety;
- trauma-informed performance review;
- and publicity safeguards against romantic taglines.

### Responsibility statement

The film must clearly preserve:

- Kanchanan did not know the woman's true identity;
- he did know that he had not asked her or the prince for an explanation;
- he concealed himself;
- he used lethal force;
- and the divine figure calls the deed cruel.

### No victim substitution

Do not centre Kanchanan's remorse so fully that:

- Udayakumaran's death becomes only the husband's lesson;
- Manimekalai's danger disappears;
- Kayasandihai becomes a plot device;
- or the royal and civic consequences vanish.

### Aftermath expansion

Any screenplay material showing Kanchanan after departure must be labelled **[INTERPRETATION]** and may not claim textual redemption.

Possible questions—not predetermined answers—include:

- Does he report Kayasandihai's death to her community?
- Does he name the killing publicly?
- Does he surrender the weapon?
- Does he seek punishment, teaching or isolation?
- Can restitution exist when the victim is dead and the harmed communities are distant?

## 15. Act-by-act character spine

### Backstory — two travellers

**External event:** Kanchanan and Kayasandihai travel from their sky-world home to the Potiya region.  
**Relationship state:** shared movement and curiosity.  
**Risk:** idealised fantasy marriage montage.  
**Performance key:** partnership before crisis.

### Curse — care without cure

**External event:** Kayasandihai loses flight and suffers insatiable hunger.  
**Choice:** remain, bring food and identify a land route toward human help.  
**Internal state:** love joined to helplessness.

### Twelve-year separation — recurring hope

**External action:** return during Indra's festival and count time toward release.  
**Internal risk:** memory of the relationship becomes more stable than the changing person.  
**Performance key:** affection with accumulating expectation.

### Return — the familiar body

**External event:** see Manimekalai in Kayasandihai's form.  
**Initial state:** relief.  
**Contradiction:** visual recognition succeeds while personal recognition fails.

### Observation — a story closes around him

**External event:** she ignores his praise and speaks intensely with Udayakumaran.  
**Choice:** interpret before asking.  
**Internal movement:** confusion → humiliation → certainty.

### Concealment — opportunity to stop

**External action:** enter with the sword and hide.  
**Moral function:** demonstrate duration and alternatives before the killing.  
**Performance key:** controlled waiting, not uncontrollable frenzy.

### Midnight — violence as answer

**External event:** the prince returns.  
**Choice:** strike from concealment.  
**Result:** irreversible death.

### Revelation — every premise fails

**External event:** the pillar figure names Manimekalai, Kayasandihai's death and Kanchanan's cruel act.  
**Internal collapse:** husband, rival, wife and justice all become different from what he believed.

### Departure — consequence without completion

**External event:** return sorrowfully to the sky world.  
**End-state:** knowledge without repair.  
**Binding rule:** do not convert departure into absolution.

- exact moment of strike;
- information delivered by the Kanthil Pavai;

### Identity warning

Manimekalai has Kayasandihai's form, not her memories, voice history or marital consent. Performance continuity must show the mismatch before Kanchanan names it as betrayal.

### Knowledge warning

Kanchanan cannot know that Kayasandihai is dead until the pillar figure tells him.

### Violence warning

Do not alter the scene into self-defence, an equal duel or accidental weapon discharge merely to make him more sympathetic.

### Fate warning

Every use of “fate” must remain paired with the text's statement that he committed a cruel deed and will face consequence.

- **AD-0144:** Kanchanan's long care for Kayasandihai is real but creates no ownership.
- **AD-0145:** Kayasandihai retains an independent life, cure, choice and death.
- **AD-0146:** Mistaken identity is built from limited observation, not supernatural madness.
- **AD-0147:** Jealousy and wounded expectation do not justify lethal certainty.
- **AD-0148:** Concealment and the opportunity to ask or withdraw remain visible.
- **AD-0149:** The killing is not converted into a heroic duel or romantic defence of marriage.
- **AD-0150:** Udayakumaran's pursuit and Kanchanan's killing remain separate responsibilities.
- **AD-0151:** Divine revelation explains fate while preserving Kanchanan's accountability.
- **AD-0152:** Sorrowful departure is not completed redemption, and later material must be logged as interpretation.

## Character thesis

Kanchanan matters because he demonstrates that care and violence can exist in the same person without one cancelling the other.

He stays with Kayasandihai when food cannot satisfy her. He searches for help, returns through years of separation and arrives expecting reunion. These actions deserve to remain true.

What also remains true is that, when the body he recognises does not behave as he expects, he does not ask whether his knowledge is wrong. He converts devotion into entitlement, uncertainty into betrayal and a weapon into judgement.

The divine voice can tell him why lives converged. It cannot make the strike unchosen.

He returns to the sky knowing that his wife is dead and that he killed a man who was not her lover. Knowledge begins. Repair does not.
